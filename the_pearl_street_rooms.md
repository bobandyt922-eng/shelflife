THE PEARL STREET ROOMS

*a short story*

By the October he stopped sleeping, August Verret had been a typesetter at the Natchez Democrat for twenty-one years, and a widower for eight, and a man who carried what he carried for eighteen, and he had told no one of any of these things in any of the ways a man tells such things, and would not in the time that remained to him to tell them.

He was forty-seven years old. He was not a large man. He had been thin as a boy and had remained thin in middle age in the way of thin men who have never eaten more than they needed and have never developed the habit of it. His hair had gone iron grey at thirty and was white at the temples now and a dirty grey above. He wore it cut short. He had the long dark eyes of his father's people, who had been Creole down at Pointe Coupee for four generations before his own father had come up to Natchez in 1862 as a young man of nineteen and had stayed. His father had been a clerk in a cotton firm on Main Street for thirty-odd years and had died in 1894, and his mother had died in 1899 of a fever, and there had been a sister but the sister had married a man from Memphis in 1903 and had died the same year of the same childbed thing that took Adèle and the child five years later, and there was no one left of August's people in the country, and there had not been since the summer of 1908.

He lived in two rooms above a saddlery on Pearl Street. The saddlery was kept by a man named Holcombe, who had been there since before August had come down to the rooms in the spring of 1895, and who said good morning to him in the alley behind the building on the days they passed each other there, and who did not say more than that. The front window of August's rooms looked across an alley at the brick wall of the warehouse opposite, six feet of air between the two buildings. A man in either window could have set his hand against the wall of the other, and August had done so once in his first year there when Holcombe's wife had asked from her window across the way whether he would mind closing his shutters at night on account of the lamp, and August had said he would not mind, and had reached his hand across the gap and shaken her hand on it, and that had been the only conversation he had ever had with her, and she had died of the influenza in 1902.

He went down to the Democrat at four o'clock six afternoons a week. The walk was three blocks from Pearl Street to the building on Main, and he made it twice a day, once down in the afternoon light and once up in the dark, and he had been making it for twenty-one years. He knew the wood of the doorways and the angles of the iron of the gates and the place at the second corner where a brick was loose in the sidewalk and had been loose since the autumn of 1903, and he stepped over it now without looking down.

He set type for the morning edition. He worked at a long bench on the second floor of the building, with the case of type on the slope it stood at in front of him, and the composing stick in his left hand, and the leads between the lines, and he had been doing the work for so long that the words moved from the copy paper at his elbow into the stick in his hand without passing through any part of him he was aware of. The foreman of the type room was a man named Aldridge, who was fifty-three years old and had been at the paper since 1888, and who had hired August in the spring of 1895 and had given him the same bench he still worked at, and who had taken, over the years, a quiet liking to him in the way the older men of that trade took quiet likings to the men they hired who turned out to do the work without complaining about it. The men in the room had called him by his last name for the first three years he was there and by his first name since, and they did not speak to him much. He was not a man other men spoke to much. He had not minded it then and did not mind it now.

His wife had been Adèle Bertrand, of Vidalia across the river in Louisiana. He had married her in the spring of 1907, when he was thirty-eight and she was twenty-six, and they had lived in the two rooms above the saddlery for the fourteen months of their marriage, and she had died in childbirth in July of 1908, and the child had died with her, and he had buried them in the Catholic cemetery on Cemetery Road. The stone over them gave both names and both dates. The date of the child's birth was the date of its death, and the child's name on the stone was the name they had chosen for a boy if it had been a boy, which it had been, but the child had not lived long enough to be called by the name in any way that would have stood in stone, and the priest had written the name down anyway, because August had asked him to, and the cutter had cut it, and the name was Pierre, after no one in particular, because Adèle had liked it.

He had kept her books on the shelf above the table in the front room. There were eleven volumes of French novels she had brought from her father's house, and three small books of poetry she had been given by a teacher when she was sixteen. He had not opened any of them in the eight years since. He had kept, in the drawer of the table, a small tin that had held cocoa once and now held the hairpins she had worn in her hair, perhaps thirty of them, and he had put the tin in the drawer on the day after the funeral and had not opened it since.

He had walked, on a July evening in 1898, along the river at the foot of the bluff below Silver Street on his way home from the paper at the end of his shift, and had seen a boy fall from the deck of a flatboat tied to the bank, and had stood twenty feet off and watched the water close over him, and had not shouted, and had not run for help, and had waited a minute, by which point the boy was gone, and had walked up the bluff by the long way so as not to pass the flatboat again. The boy had been the son of a deckhand. The boy's name was Thomas Beaufort. He was eight years old. August had set the brief about the body two days afterward at his own bench, and the brief had given the boy's name and the boy's age and the fact that he was colored, and the fact that no foul play was suspected. He had set every word of it. He had not told anyone what he had seen, then or since. He had not told Adèle in all the years she had lived after that night, and he had not told anyone in the eight years since she died. He could not swim and never could. There was that. There was also the other thing, which was that he had not stayed on the bank even until someone came. He had walked home. He had washed his hands at the basin and eaten the cold supper Adèle had left him under a cloth. He had gone to bed beside his wife. He had slept that night and every night for eighteen years.

The autumn of 1916 came on dry and warm. The cane fires began in the bottoms below the bluff in the last week of September, and the smoke of them came up the river road and into the town and into the wool of every coat on Main Street, and there were men at the lunch counters who remarked on it as they remarked on it every October. August did not particularly remark on it. He had set, on a Friday evening at the beginning of the month, the obituary of a man named Tellman who had been a tenant farmer on a place up the road toward the Ravenel land, and who had died on his own gallery in his own chair, and August had thought, in the way he thought about the names he set, that the name had a hard sound to it for the name of a man who had died easy. He had set the brief. He had moved on to the next.

On the evening of Wednesday the eleventh of October, August came into the type room at the usual hour and took his place at his bench and pulled the case toward him in the usual way, and he saw that there was, lying on the slope of the case at the upper right where the figures and the punctuation were kept, a single piece of type out of place. It was a lowercase r. He picked it up. He turned it between his thumb and his forefinger. It was from a font he had not used in some years, a small italic he had set once for a wedding announcement when he was new at the paper and had not set since, because the paper had not run italics for some time except in the legal notices, and the legals were a different font. He held the slug for a moment in his fingers. Then he put it in his pocket, because he did not, at that moment, know where in the case it ought to go back, and he meant to think about it later.

He did not think about it later. He worked his shift. He came home at twelve past midnight. He did not, on that night, sleep more than two hours.

***

He thought, at first, that it was the heat.

The thirteenth of October had come up warm in the way October could come up warm in that country, a wet warmth that settled into the rooms above Pearl Street and did not lift in the night, and he lay in his bed with the sheet pushed down to his ankles and the window open to the alley, and the cane smoke came in over the sill and lay on the boards of the floor and on the small braided rug Adèle had brought from her father's house, and he did not sleep. He had slept until four in the morning. He had woken without any reason he could name. He had lain in the dark and waited for the sleep to come back, and it had not, and at half past five he had risen and put on his trousers and gone to the front window and looked at the brick wall of the warehouse across the alley until the first grey of the morning came up out of the river, and then he had lit the small stove and made coffee and sat at the table and drunk it.

He thought it was the heat for two days. Then he thought it was the smoke, which seemed thicker than it had been in other Octobers, although he could not have said by how much. Then he thought it was the coffee, of which he had been drinking five cups a day at the bench for as long as he had been at the paper, and he tried, for one afternoon, to do without it, and felt by six o'clock as though there were a small iron band tightening at his temples, and he gave it up and drank a cup at the bench and felt the band loosen, and went on with his work.

The slug of the lowercase r was in the pocket of his coat. He had forgotten about it. He found it again on the Saturday morning while he was emptying his pockets at the table before brushing the coat, and he looked at it and turned it over once and set it down on the table beside the tin of hairpins and the few coins he had carried home the night before. He meant to take it back to the paper that afternoon and find the right place for it. He did not. He went down to the paper at four o'clock and worked his shift, and when he came home at midnight and emptied his pockets the slug was in his pocket again, although he had not put it there. He looked at it for a moment. Then he set it on the table beside the other one.

There were two of them on the table now. He looked at them for some while. They were the same letter from the same font, identical in every particular he could see. He had not picked up two of them at the bench. He had picked up one. He knew this the way a man knows the weight of his own coat in his hand. He sat down at the table and looked at them, and after a few minutes he picked them up together and put them in the drawer of the table, on top of the tin of hairpins, and shut the drawer.

He went to the apothecary on State Street on the Monday afternoon before his shift, which was the sixteenth of October. The apothecary was a man named Lassiter, who had been on State Street since before August had come to live on Pearl, and who knew him by his face if not by his name, and who asked him what he wanted.

I am not sleeping, August said.

How long.

A week.

A whole week.

About that.

Lassiter looked at him over the top of his small wire spectacles. He was a thin grey man of sixty who had been in the trade for forty years and had seen a great many men come into his shop on a Monday afternoon and say what August had just said. He turned to the shelves behind the counter and took down a small brown bottle and set it on the counter between them.

Paregoric, he said. A teaspoon at bedtime. No more than that. It will put you under by half past nine. You will sleep eight hours. You will wake up clear. Do not take it more than four nights in a row. After that, leave it alone for a week. Do you understand me.

Yes.

Twenty-five cents.

August paid him. He took the bottle home and set it on the table beside the lamp. He went down to the paper at four. He worked his shift. He came home at twelve past midnight. He took a teaspoon of the paregoric in a glass of water and went to bed.

He slept three hours. He woke at half past three in the morning. He lay in the dark and waited. The sleep did not come back. He rose at five. He sat at the table and looked at the brown bottle in the lamplight and thought about it for some while. He did not take a second teaspoon.

The errors began on the Tuesday.

He set, that evening, a brief about a small fire at a feed store on Franklin Street, and he set the address as 1898 Franklin, which was not an address, and he did not notice the error until he had pulled the proof and Aldridge had come past the bench on his round at half past eight and looked over August's shoulder and put his finger on the line.

August.

Yes.

That is not the address.

He looked. He saw it. He saw, also, that it was the year of the drowning, set in lead under his hand, and he felt the small cold thing he had felt twice in his life when he had been very close to dying without having known he was, once when he was eleven years old and a horse had come past him in a lane at a gallop and missed him by perhaps eight inches, and once when he was thirty-three and had not seen, in the dark, the open side of a cellar on Main Street and had stopped at the edge of it without knowing how he had stopped. He took the slug of the eight out of the line and replaced it. The address now read 198 Franklin. He looked at it. It was still wrong. The address was 138 Franklin. He looked at Aldridge.

I am sorry.

It is all right. Make sure.

He made sure. Aldridge moved on down the bench.

He set, on the Wednesday, the wedding announcement of a woman named Hattie Pemberton, and he set the bride's name as Hattei. He did not catch it. The proof reader caught it. The proof reader was a boy of nineteen named Lee, who had been at the paper for a year, and who came to August's bench with the proof in his hand and stood at the corner of the bench and waited until August had finished the line he was setting, and then he laid the proof on the bench and pointed to the name without speaking.

August looked at it.

I am sorry, Lee.

It is no matter. It happens.

It does not happen to me.

It does to all of us, Mr. Verret. Once in a while.

August fixed the name. Lee took the proof and went back to his table at the far end of the room. August set the rest of the brief and the next one and the next one. He did not make another error that night.

He set, on the Thursday, the date of a county supervisor's meeting as the nineteenth of October instead of the twentieth. He caught this one himself, on the second reading, and corrected it. He did not show it to Aldridge.

He set, on the Friday, a brief about a man who had died in Vidalia of a heart attack at the age of fifty-three, and he set the man's last name as Bertrand, which had been Adèle's father's name and was not the name in the copy. The name in the copy was Boudreaux. He stared at the slug in his stick for some while. He had not, to his memory, picked Bertrand out of the case. He had picked Boudreaux. He could not, in the moment, have said how the one had become the other. He set Boudreaux. He pulled the proof. He read it twice. It was correct. He sent it on.

He came home that night at twelve past midnight. He sat at the table. He did not light the second lamp. The single small lamp on the table threw the yellow circle on the boards. He opened the drawer of the table. The two slugs of the lowercase r were on top of the tin of hairpins, where he had put them. He took them out and laid them on the table.

There were three of them now.

He sat for some while looking at them. Then he put all three back in the drawer, and shut the drawer, and went to bed, and did not sleep at all.

***

The dream came on the Saturday night.

He had worked his shift on the Saturday, which was the twenty-first of October, and he had eaten nothing since breakfast except a piece of bread at five o'clock at his bench, and he had come home at midnight and had taken another teaspoon of the paregoric, and he had gone to bed at half past twelve and had fallen asleep almost at once, in the way a man falls asleep when his body has decided that the matter is no longer in his hands.

He dreamed he was at his bench.

He was not at his bench in the daylight of an afternoon shift. He was at his bench at some hour he could not place, in a light that was neither the light of the gas fixtures overhead nor the light of any window, a kind of low even grey light that came from nowhere and threw no shadow. The case of type was in front of him. The composing stick was in his left hand. The copy paper at his elbow was blank. He looked at the blank paper for some while and then he looked up.

There was a man standing across the workroom at the far end of the long bench, perhaps twenty feet from him.

The man was not Aldridge. The man was not Lee. The man was not any of the men who worked the room. The man was wet. His clothes were wet through and clung to him, and his hair was wet and hung against his forehead, and a small dark pool had formed on the boards of the floor beneath his feet and was spreading slowly outward in the careful unhurried way water spreads on a flat surface. The man did not move. The man was looking at August across the bench.

August could not see the man's face. He could see that the man had a face, and that the face was turned toward him, and that the eyes of the face were on his eyes, but he could not see the features of the face. The light did not fall on it in any way that allowed the features to come forward. The face was the face of a man, only that. It was not the face of a child.

August stood at the bench with the composing stick in his hand. He did not move. The man did not move. The pool on the floor went on spreading. After what in the dream was perhaps a minute, the man lifted his right hand slowly from his side and held it out in front of him, palm up, the way a man holds out his hand to show that there is nothing in it. The hand was empty. The hand was wet. There was nothing in it. The man stood with his hand held out for some while, and then he lowered the hand to his side again, and then he turned, slowly, and walked the length of the room toward the door at the far end, and his wet footprints went after him on the boards, and he went through the door, and the door did not open. He went through it the way a man goes through air. He was gone.

August woke.

He woke at three in the morning by the small clock on the table beside the bed. He was not sweating. His heart was not racing. He was as calm as he had ever been on waking. He lay in the bed and looked at the ceiling and the ceiling was the ceiling of his room above Pearl Street, and the ceiling had a small water stain in the corner above the window that had been there since 1903 when Holcombe had had a leak in the roof of the saddlery, and the water stain was where it had been, and the bed was the bed, and his hand on the sheet was his hand. He lay for some while. Then he rose and went to the front window and opened the shutter and looked across the alley at the brick wall of the warehouse, and the wall was the wall, and the night was the night, and the cane smoke had thinned in the last hour as it did toward morning, and he could smell, beneath the smoke, the colder smell of the river coming up out of the bottoms.

He did not go back to bed. He sat at the table in the front room until first light. He did not light the lamp.

The dream came again on the Tuesday night.

It was the same dream. The man was wet. The pool spread. The face did not come forward. The hand was held out empty. The man went through the door without opening it. August woke. He looked at the ceiling. The water stain was where it had been.

He began, after that, to walk.

He could not sleep before three or four in the morning, and he could not always sleep then, and the lying in the bed had become a thing he could not do for more than half an hour at a stretch, and so he rose at one or two in the morning and dressed and went down the stairs past the door of the saddlery and out into Pearl Street and turned south and walked. He walked the bluff. The bluff above the river ran north and south the length of the old town, and there was a path along the top of it from the cemetery road down past the foot of State Street, and at one in the morning there was nobody on it. He walked from one end of it to the other and back, and the river below him was very dark, and the lights on the far side at Vidalia were a thin scatter of yellow points across the water, and he walked at a steady pace and did not think of anything in particular, by which is meant that he thought of the things he had been thinking of since the eleventh of October and had not yet found a way to set down.

He passed, on the Wednesday night, a young couple coming out of the door of the hotel at the corner of Main Street. The man had his arm in the woman's arm. The woman was laughing at something the man had said. They were perhaps twenty-five years old. They did not see him. He stepped into the shadow of the building across the street as they came out, although he had not meant to step into the shadow, and he stood there and watched them go up the street arm in arm, and the laughing of the woman carried back to him in the still night for a hundred feet and then was lost. He stood in the shadow for some while after they were gone. The not looking at them while they passed him had been a thing he had not known was in him, and the doing of it had cost him something he could not name, and he stood in the shadow and felt the cost of it for some minutes, and then he went on.

The footsteps in the hall began on the Thursday night.

He came home at twelve past midnight, as he had come home for twenty-one years, and he climbed the stairs to his rooms in the dark, as he had climbed them for twenty-one years, and he went into the front room and lit the lamp and sat at the table for some while, and then at perhaps one o'clock he heard a step in the hall outside his door.

The hall ran the length of the second floor of the building above the saddlery, and there were four doors on the hall, of which August's was the second from the front of the building, and the other three were the rooms of three other tenants. There was a clerk at the bank named Pritchard who lived in the front room. There was a widow named Mrs. Sims who lived in the third room and who took in sewing. There was a man named Doyle in the fourth room who worked on the river and was gone for weeks at a time. The hall had a board near August's door that creaked under any weight, and the board had been creaking since 1899, and any of the three other tenants coming home at any hour of the night made the board creak as they passed.

August heard the board creak. He did not think anything of it. He thought it was Pritchard coming up late.

He heard a second step. He heard a third. The steps stopped outside his door.

He listened. He did not move from the chair. The steps did not move on. They did not move back. They stood, whoever they were, outside his door, for what was by the small clock on the table perhaps a minute.

He heard a man clear his throat.

The sound was very close. It was the sound of a man clearing his throat on the other side of a door, standing with his face perhaps two feet from the door, in the way a man clears his throat before he raises his hand to knock. There was no knock. There was no second sound after the first. There was only the throat being cleared, once, softly, and then nothing.

August sat at the table. He did not move. The lamp on the table burned without flicker. He looked at the door. The door was the door. The brass of the handle caught the lamplight in the way the brass of any handle catches the lamplight, and there was no shadow under the door that should not have been there, and there was no second clearing of the throat.

He sat for a long time. Perhaps it was ten minutes. Perhaps it was twenty. Then he rose from the chair and crossed the room and put his hand on the handle of the door and turned it and pulled the door open.

The hall was empty. The lamp on the wall at the head of the stairs had been turned down for the night by the woman who came in to do the hall, and it threw a small dim light along the boards, and the boards were empty, and the doors of the other three rooms were closed, and there was nothing in the hall, and nothing on the stairs, and the second board, the one that creaked, did not creak as August stepped on it himself, going out to look up the hall in both directions, although it had creaked under his foot every other time he had ever stepped on it, in the seventeen years it had been creaking. He stood in the hall in his shirtsleeves for some while. He went back into his room. He shut the door. He sat at the table.

The next morning, in the daylight, he stepped on the board again on his way down to do his marketing. The board creaked under his foot in the way it had creaked for seventeen years.

He set, on the Friday evening, a notice for an estate sale in town, and he set the date of the sale correctly, and the address of the house correctly, and the name of the executor correctly, and he made no errors at all in his work that night. He worked at his bench as he had worked at it for twenty-one years. Aldridge came past at half past eight on his round and looked at the proofs August had pulled and put his hand briefly on August's shoulder as he went by, which was a thing Aldridge had done perhaps four times in twenty-one years, and which August understood to mean that the man had seen what was happening to him and would not speak of it, and that the bench was his for as long as he could keep working at it, and that there would be no harder conversation than this one for some while yet.

August thanked him without looking up from the stick.

He came home at twelve past midnight. He took the slugs of the lowercase r out of the drawer and laid them on the table to count them.

There were five.

***

The last week of October came on cooler than the third week, with a wind that came in from the northwest at night and stripped the willow oaks along the bluff of the first of their leaves, and the cane fires went on at the far edge of the bottomland but the smoke of them was thinner now, and the air in the rooms above Pearl Street was colder than it had been for some time, and August closed the front window for the first time in a month on the Saturday night and did not open it again.

He had stopped trying to sleep in the bed. He had taken to lying in his clothes on top of the counterpane with his coat folded over him in the place of a second blanket, and he would lie that way for an hour and then rise and sit at the table for an hour, and then he would go out into the street and walk. He walked the bluff. He walked the cemetery road as far as the gate of the Catholic cemetery and stood at the gate for some while looking through the iron at the rows of stones in the dark, and then he walked back. He did not go in. He had not been in the cemetery in eight years and he did not go in now.

He saw, on the Sunday night, a figure standing in the alley across from the saddlery.

He had come out at half past two and had walked up Pearl to the corner and had turned to look back at his own window, in the way he had been turning to look back at it on his walks since the dream of the wet man, although he could not have said what he was looking for when he looked back at his window. The window was dark. The shutter was open. The brick wall of the warehouse opposite the window was visible from the corner in the thin grey of the gaslight at the head of Pearl Street. The figure was standing at the foot of the warehouse wall on the warehouse side of the alley, in the place where a man would stand if he wanted to look up into August's window without being seen from the street.

The figure was small. It was not the height of a grown man. It was the height of a child of perhaps eight or nine years old.

August stood at the corner for some while. He did not move toward the figure. He did not move away. The figure did not move. The figure was looking up at the window of August's room, which it could see from where it stood and which August could not see from where he stood, because the angle of his own corner did not allow it. After perhaps a minute the figure turned its head slowly and looked down Pearl Street, not at August at the corner but past him, in the direction of the river, and held the look for some while, and then turned its head back to the window. August stood at the corner and watched.

A buggy came up Pearl from the direction of the river. The horse came up at a walk. The man driving the buggy was a man August did not know, with a hat pulled down against the wind, and he did not look at August at the corner, and he did not look into the alley, and the buggy went past August at the corner and on up Pearl toward Main, and the sound of the hooves carried for some while in the cold air. When August looked back into the alley the figure was gone. The alley was empty. The brick wall of the warehouse was empty. The window of his own room was the window of his own room.

He did not go up the stairs that night. He walked until first light. He came home at half past six in the morning and sat at the table and drank coffee until it was time to go down to the paper.

He saw, on the Monday afternoon, a face at the lunch counter at a place on Franklin Street where he ate sometimes before his shift.

He had gone in at three o'clock and had taken a seat at the counter, which had eight stools, of which four were occupied, and he had ordered a plate of beans and a piece of cornbread and a cup of coffee, and he had eaten in the silent way he ate in the public places of the town, with his coat on and his hat on the stool beside him and his eyes on the plate in front of him. He had finished the plate and had pushed it forward on the counter and had taken up the coffee and had drunk perhaps half of it when he had lifted his eyes and looked along the counter to his right.

There were three men at the counter to his right. The first was a man named Coker who kept the livery on the next street and whom August knew by sight. The second was a man August did not know, in the rough wool coat of a man who worked on the river. The third was a Black man at the far end of the counter, in the dress of a deckhand, with a heavy moustache and a cap on the stool beside him and the long flat face of a man who had spent forty years in the weather. He was eating beans. He did not look up. He ate slowly. He had not looked up at any time since August had come into the place.

August looked at him.

He could not place the face. He could not say, with any certainty, that the face was the face of any man he had ever seen. He had not, to his knowledge, ever seen the face of the deckhand whose son had drowned at the foot of Silver Street in July of 1898, and he did not know whether the deckhand was still alive, or whether he had been on the river all these years, or whether he had gone, in the eighteen years since, to some other place. He looked at the face for perhaps five seconds. The man did not look up.

August set down his coffee. He left twenty-five cents on the counter beside the cup, and a nickel for the boy who had brought the plate, and he took his hat from the stool and put it on, and he went out of the place without looking at the man at the far end of the counter again. The bell at the door rang as he went out. The man at the far end of the counter did not look up.

August walked the three blocks to the paper. He went up the stairs. He took his place at the bench. He worked his shift.

He set, on the Tuesday evening, perhaps fifteen briefs, of which one was a notice of a court date and one was a death notice and one was an item about a new shipment of fall millinery at a store on State Street, and he set them all without error, in the way a man who has been at a trade for twenty-one years can set type without error even when he has not slept for sixteen nights, because the hands of the man have at that point taken over from any other part of him and will set what the eye reads off the copy paper without the rest of him having to be involved. He came home at twelve past midnight. He did not sleep.

He went down to the paper on the Wednesday at four o'clock and the type room was different when he came into it.

He could not have said how it was different. The men were the men. The bench was the bench. The light of the gas fixtures overhead was the light of the gas fixtures overhead. There was a thing in the air of the room, however, that had not been in the air of the room the night before, and August stopped just inside the door and stood for a moment, and Aldridge looked up from his table and saw him and came across the room to him.

A bad thing has happened down the road, Aldridge said. There has been a killing.

Who.

A young man named Ravenel. Out of the country south of here, at a place called Bellechasse. He was shot from a rise above the river road yesterday evening. The Beaumont boy from Glenmary has been taken in. There has been correspondence between the families for some weeks on a piece of land that lies between the two places, and it has gone bad. The boy was twenty-two years old. The father brought the boy in himself this morning, in his own buggy, and stood with him in the sheriff's office while the boy made his statement, and the boy is in the cell now and will be there for some while. The story has come in this afternoon from a man who was at the courthouse. I have copy for a brief. Will you set it.

I will set it, August said.

Aldridge brought him the copy paper. August took it to his bench and laid it on the slope of the case and read it once, and then he read it a second time, and then he began to set.

He set the name of the dead man. He set the age. He set the name of the place. He set the date. He set the words *shot from a rise above the road.* He set the name of the boy who had done it and the name of the father of the boy who had done it. He set the address of the courthouse and the date of the spring term of the court. He set the small line at the end of the brief that said the dead man had been the only son. He set the brief in eight minutes.

He pulled the proof. He read the proof. The proof was clean. He sent the proof to Lee.

He went back to the case. He picked up the next piece of copy. He set the next brief. He set the brief after that. He worked his shift. He did not, at any point in the rest of the shift, think about the brief he had set at half past four, in any way that he would afterward be able to recall.

He came home at twelve past midnight.

He climbed the stairs to his rooms in the dark. He did not light the lamp. He sat at the table in the front room in his coat with his hands on the boards of the table, and he sat that way for some while. Then he reached out his right hand and pulled the drawer of the table open, and he reached into the drawer past the slugs of the lowercase r, of which there were now seven, and he took out the small cocoa tin that had held Adèle's hairpins for eight years and four months, and he set the tin on the table in front of him.

He looked at the tin for some while.

He worked the lid of the tin loose with his thumb. The lid had not been opened for a long time and the lid did not want to come up, but he worked at it patiently in the way a man works at a thing that he has not done in many years and that he is in no hurry to finish, and after a minute or perhaps two the lid came free, and he set the lid on the table beside the tin and he looked into the tin.

The hairpins were in the tin. They were the dull brown of dry hairpins that had been in a cocoa tin for eight years. There were perhaps thirty of them, lying in the bottom of the tin in the loose tangled way they had lain in the tin when he had put them there on the afternoon after the funeral. He looked at them. He did not touch them. He sat at the table in his coat in the dark and looked into the open tin in the lamplight that was not on, by which is meant in the grey light that came in from the alley through the open shutter of the front window, and the hairpins were in the tin, and they were what they had been, and that was all.

After some while he put the lid back on the tin and he put the tin back in the drawer and he shut the drawer, and he sat at the table in the dark until first light.

***

The week that followed was a week he did not afterward remember in the way a man remembers a week.

He went down to the paper at four o'clock on the Thursday and worked his shift. He went down on the Friday. He went down on the Saturday and on the Monday and on the days that followed it. He set type on each of those evenings. He did not make any errors that Aldridge or Lee found, and he did not catch any errors of his own, and whether this was because he made none or because the part of him that caught errors had stopped working he could not have said. The bench knew the work and the hands knew the bench and the rest of him sat behind his eyes at some distance from both, watching them go.

He did not sleep through any of the nights of that week. He did not lie down. He sat at the table in the dark in the hours between his shifts and his walks, and he walked the bluff at one and at three and at five in the morning, and the cold came up off the river and went through the wool of his coat and into the bones of him, and he did not feel the cold particularly. He ate, at the lunch counter on Franklin where he had seen the face on the Monday, on perhaps four of the days of the week that followed, and the deckhand was not there on any of them, and the boy who worked the place asked him on one of those days if he was unwell, and he said no, he was only tired, and he paid for his plate and went on.

He set, on the Saturday evening of the eleventh of November, a wedding announcement and two birth notices and four briefs of various small kinds, and he set, at half past nine, the death notice of a man named Étienne Ravenel, who had died on the morning of the previous Wednesday, and who had been the great-uncle of the young Ravenel August had set the brief about ten days before, and who had been laid in the ground at Bellechasse on the Friday afternoon. August read the copy. He set the type. He set the name of the man twice, once at the head of the notice and once at the foot. He set the dates. He set the names of the surviving family, which were Henri Ravenel and Cécile Ravenel and Sylvie Ravenel. He set the brief in six minutes. He pulled the proof. The proof was clean. He sent it on. He did not think, while he was setting it, about the brief he had set the week before. He did not, in fact, think anything at all while he was setting it. He set it and he moved on to the next.

He came home at twelve past midnight on the Saturday. He sat at the table.

He went out at half past two in the morning, which was now the Sunday morning of the twelfth of November, and he walked down Pearl to the corner of State and turned and went down State toward the bluff, and he walked the path along the top of the bluff in the direction of the foot of Silver Street, and at the head of the path that went down the face of the bluff to the under-the-hill he stopped and looked out at the river in the dark and the lights at Vidalia were a thin scatter of yellow points the way they had been every other night he had stood there, and the river below him was very dark and the wind had gone down with the night, and there was no sound up out of the bottoms but the small clear sound of the water on the bank below.

He went down the path.

He had gone down the path perhaps a hundred times in his life, in the daylight, on errands of one kind or another, and twice he had gone down it at night, both times in the company of other men. He went down it now alone. The path was narrow and the dirt of it was cold under the soles of his shoes and the dirt was loose in places where the rain had washed it, and he went down at the same steady pace he had walked the bluff, and at the bottom the path came out onto the flat between the foot of the bluff and the river, and there were warehouses to his right and the river to his left, and there were no men in the street and no lamps lit and the place was as quiet as it ever was.

He walked north along the flat. He walked for perhaps a quarter of a mile. He came, at the end of that quarter mile, to the place where in July of 1898 a flatboat had been tied to a low post in the bank, and where a boy of eight had fallen from the deck of it and had gone under and had not come up. The post was still there in the bank. It was the same post. The wood of it had gone grey in eighteen years and the iron ring at the top of it was the same iron ring, and there was no boat tied to it now, and the bank in front of it was empty, and the water came up to the bank and lay against it the way the water of any river lies against any bank in the dark when the wind has gone down.

He stopped at the post.

He took off his coat. He folded the coat in half and laid it on the ground at the foot of the post. He took off his hat and laid the hat on the coat. He sat down on the coat and unlaced his shoes and took them off and set them side by side on the coat beside the hat. He took his pocket watch out of the watch pocket of his vest and laid it on the shoes. He took the pay envelope out of the inside pocket of the coat, which still held the cash from his Saturday pay, and laid the envelope on top of the watch. He stood up.

He did not look at the coat or at the things on the coat. He did not look at the bluff behind him or at the river in front of him or at the lights at Vidalia. He looked at the post. The post had a small mark in the grey wood near the top, perhaps eight inches below the iron ring, which was the mark of a rope that had been tied there once and had cut into the wood with the weight of a boat, and the mark had been in the wood for longer than eighteen years.

He stepped down off the bank into the water.

The water was cold. It came to the middle of his calves at the first step, and to his knees at the second, and to the middle of his thighs at the third. The bottom under his feet was the soft give of the river silt, in which his feet sank an inch with each step. He kept walking. The water came to his waist. The water came to his chest. He could not swim. He had never been able to swim. He took two more steps and the bottom went away from him and the river took him, and he went under, and the water closed over the top of his head in the careful unhurried way water closes over the top of any man's head, and he did not come up.

The body of August Verret was found at twenty minutes past three on the Sunday afternoon by a man fishing from a skiff two miles downstream from the foot of Silver Street, in a place where the current had carried him into a stand of willow on the Mississippi bank. The man poled in and looked at the face and did not know it. He took the body up out of the water with some difficulty and laid it in the bottom of the skiff and rowed up to the foot of the bluff and got two other men to help him carry it up the path, and the body was brought to the office of the coroner on State Street in the late afternoon, and the coroner identified him by the contents of the trouser pockets, which held a key to a room above the saddlery on Pearl Street and a single piece of type, which was a lowercase r from a small italic font, and which the coroner did not know the meaning of and did not ask anyone about, and which was eventually given over to the woman who came in to clear the rooms above Pearl Street and was thrown out with the rest of what was in the drawer of the table in the front room.

The brief ran in the Democrat on the Tuesday morning. It was set on the Monday evening by Lee, who was the proof reader and who could set type as well as read it, because Aldridge had decided that the brief about August Verret would not be set by anyone in the room who had worked beside the man for more than a year. The brief read as follows.

August Verret, 47, of Pearl Street, employed for twenty-one years as a compositor at this paper, was found drowned in the river below the bluff on Sunday afternoon. The cause is supposed to be accidental. Mr. Verret had been a widower since 1908 and leaves no family. Funeral arrangements will be announced by the parish of St. Mary on Cemetery Road.

Lee set the brief in five minutes. He pulled the proof. The proof was clean. He sent the proof to Aldridge, who read it without expression and laid it on the desk and did not speak, and Lee went back to the case to set the next item.

He needed, for the next item, a lowercase r. He reached into the compartment of the case where the lowercase r was kept, in the font that the briefs of the paper were set in, which was not the small italic that had ended in August Verret's trouser pocket but the regular roman face the paper had used for forty years. He took out a slug. He set it. He reached for another. The compartment was full. There were perhaps a hundred and twenty of the lowercase r in the compartment, in the regular roman face, which was the number of them that had been in the compartment that morning and the day before and for many days before that.

He went on with his work.

The rooms above Pearl Street were cleared on the Thursday by a woman named Mrs. Hennessey, who was paid by the parish of St. Mary to do such work when there was no family to do it, and who took the books and the few clothes and the small kitchen things to the parish hall for the rummage, and who threw out the contents of the drawer of the table in the front room into a tin pail along with the contents of the kitchen cupboard and the stove, and who carried the pail down the back stairs and emptied it into the alley behind the saddlery, where the cart came on the Friday morning and took it away. The tin of hairpins went into the alley with the rest. The slugs of the lowercase r went into the alley with the rest. The cart came and took them.

The river goes on.

The bluff stands. The cane fires of October came on again the next year and the year after that and every October for some years, and the smoke of them came up through the country and into the wool of every coat in Adams County, and there were men at the lunch counters who remarked on it as they remarked on it every October, and after a generation there were no men at the lunch counters who had ever set type beside August Verret or seen him walk up Pearl Street at twelve past midnight in his coat, and the brick wall of the warehouse across the alley from the window of the room he had lived in was torn down in 1934 to make room for a parking lot, and the saddlery closed in 1939, and the building was sold in 1947 and the upper rooms were cleared out and let to a man who worked at the cotton press and who knew nothing of the man who had lived in them before. There was no one left who remembered. There was no one left who could have said what had been in the rooms or what had not. The story, such as it was, went out of the country in the way most stories go out of the country, which is quietly, and over time, and without anyone alive being able to mark the moment of its going.
