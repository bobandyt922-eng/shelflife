# THE LONG WATCH

## I

By the autumn of his fifty-fourth year, Théodore Bailliard had been waiting forty-five years for his sleep to begin to leave him, and on the first Sunday in October of 1911 it began, and he was lying in the bed his great-grandfather had brought up from Pointe Coupee in 1815 when it began, and his wife was asleep beside him, and the long-case clock at the foot of the bed that had been wound every Sunday for a hundred and thirty-one years by a Bailliard man was striking three.

He counted the strokes. He had been counting the strokes of clocks for as long as he had any memory of counting anything. The clock struck three and was quiet, and the silence after the third stroke was the silence he had been listening for since he was nine years old, when his father had sat him down in the shop on Franklin Street and told him plainly what he was, and what would happen to him in his fifty-fourth year, and how he should comport himself in the meantime.

His father had said, that day in the shop, that there was no use in dreading what could not be moved. He had said that a clockmaker was a man who took up an instrument and made it tell time, and that the time it told was the time the maker had given to it, and not the time of God or of any other thing in the world, and that the time which was given to a Bailliard man was the time he would have, and a Bailliard man would have fifty-four years of it, and after the fifty-four years a small and uncountable remainder, and the remainder was not for working with. He had said that this had been the case as far back as the family records went, which was 1821, and likely further back than that, although the records before 1821 were thin and the man before 1821 had been buried in Pointe Coupee under a stone with no dates.

His father had said also, that day, that the sleep would begin to leave him in the autumn of his fifty-fourth year, and that he would know it for what it was when it began, and that he was not to be afraid of it. His father had been thirty-nine then, and Théodore had looked at his father across the workbench in the shop on Franklin Street with the slow understanding of a child who has just been told the shape of his life, and had said nothing, because there had been nothing to say. His father had died in November of 1881, in the chair beside the long-case clock, on a Wednesday night, and the dying had taken him within the year, and Théodore had been twenty-four years old and had been the one who found him in the morning with Léonie, who had been a girl of twenty-three from the next house and who had come over to the Bailliard kitchen because she had heard, through her wall, the long uninterrupted silence of a house that had stopped listening for a man.

Théodore lay now in the same bed where his father had died and where his grandfather had died and where his great-grandfather had died, and he listened for the silence after the three strokes, and the silence held, and he understood that the autumn of his fifty-fourth year had begun.

He did not wake Léonie. He did not move. He lay on his back with his hands folded on his chest above the blanket the way the dead are laid, although he was not yet dead and would not be for some time, and he listened to the breathing of his wife of twenty-eight years beside him, and he listened to the small sounds the long-case clock made in the second after the three strokes when the strike-train was settling back into the wait for the half hour, and he listened to the river at the foot of the bluff a quarter mile to the west, although the river itself was not audible from his bedroom and what he was listening to was the absence of it.

He did not sleep again that night. He lay until first light. The clock struck the half hour, and the four, and the half hour, and the five, and at the five he rose from the bed and went down to the kitchen and made his coffee at the stove without waking Léonie, and he sat at the table in the kitchen with his coffee in front of him and watched the windows turn from black to grey to the cold rust of an October dawn over the bluff, and he did not move except to lift the cup, and when Léonie came down at half past six he was sitting in the same posture and the cup was empty and the stove had gone out, and she stopped in the doorway and looked at him and he looked back at her, and she did not need to ask.

"It has begun," he said.

"When."

"At three."

She came into the kitchen. She did not go to him. She went to the stove and laid a hand on the iron and felt it cold, and she opened the front of it and laid in two pieces of pine and a piece of cypress and lit the fire from a paper twist she had been keeping in a tin on the shelf for that purpose, and she set the kettle on, and she stood with her back to him for a moment with her hand on the rail of the stove, and then she turned and came and sat down across from him.

"How much."

"Two hours, perhaps. A little less."

"Two hours from what time."

"From after the lamp."

She nodded. She did not weep. She had not wept in twenty-eight years on this subject and she would not begin in the autumn of his fifty-fourth year with the kettle on the stove and the dawn coming up over the bluff. She was a small dark-haired woman, gone grey at the temples in the manner of her own mother, and she had been raised in the house next door to the Bailliards and had known what they were since she was a child, and she had sat at the kitchen table of her own mother's house at the age of fifteen and made the decision she had been making ever since, which was that she would marry the boy in the next yard and walk into the house with him and stand alongside him for as long as the inheritance allowed her to stand, and that she would not afterward have wished to have made a different decision, and the decision had held in her without weakening for thirty-eight years.

"There is the clock at Saint Mary's to be wound this morning," she said.

"I know."

"Will you go."

"I will."

"And after that."

"There is the courthouse on Tuesday. The Carrington house on Wednesday. The Lessley house on Friday. The Ravenel place at Bellechasse on the second Thursday."

"You will keep your circuit."

"I will keep my circuit, Léonie. The clocks do not stop because a man's sleep has thinned."

"No."

"They are the work."

"I know."

He drank the last cold swallow of his coffee. He set the cup on the table. He looked across at her, at the hands folded on the cloth in front of her in the way her own mother had folded them at the same table, and he was thinking, in that moment, of his father, and of the way his father had walked out of the kitchen of this house on the morning after the first night his own sleep had thinned, in October of 1881, and had gone down to the shop on Franklin Street and had opened the shop and had set out his tools and had begun the day's work as if nothing had been taken from him in the night, although a thing had been taken from him in the night, and he had carried the taking of it for the weeks that remained to him without ever speaking of it again.

"I will go down to the shop," he said.

"Yes."

"I will be home for supper."

"Yes."

He rose. He went up the stairs to the bedroom and dressed in the suit he wore to the shop, which was a dark grey suit his father had ordered for him from a tailor in New Orleans in 1879 and which had been re-cut twice in the intervening years, and he came back down the stairs and took his hat from the peg in the front hall, and he opened the front door and went out into the cold rust of the morning, and he walked down Pearl Street and turned onto Franklin Street, and he walked the three blocks to the shop, and he opened the shop, and he set out his tools, and he began the day's work.

The shop stood on Franklin Street three doors east of the cotton exchange, in a yellow brick building built in 1853 by a man named Halbert who had owned it until 1869 and whose name was still cut in the cornice above the door, although the gold leaf that had once filled the cuts was gone now and the cuts were the colour of soot. The plate-glass window of the shop bore the legend BAILLIARD CLOCKS in gold leaf his father had laid down in 1864, and which had been touched up twice since, and which still held. Inside, the shop was the long shop it had always been, with the workbench under the window and the gas lamp on the iron arm above the bench and the rows of small drawers behind the bench in which the parts were kept, and the clocks in for repair on the shelves above the drawers, and the stove near the back, and the back wall lined with the long-case clocks in their walnut and oak and cherry, and at the back corner, in the dimmest part of the shop, the long-case clock his great-grandfather Joseph had begun in the year of his own dying and had not finished. The clock had no hands. The clock had no weights. The clock had no striking train. It had only the case and the dial, and the dial was bare brass with the hour numerals cut into it and no other marks, and Théodore had stood in front of it three or four times a year for thirty years and had not added to it, although the tools and parts to finish it were in the drawers behind the bench and had been there since 1821, and a man who knew clocks could have finished it in a week. His father had not finished it. His grandfather had not finished it. The clock had been waiting for ninety years, and Théodore did not know whether he would finish it in his own remainder, although he had begun, in the recent months, to look at it more often than he had used to.

Reuben came in at seven, as he had come in at seven for seventeen years, and he set down his lunch pail by the back door and he hung up his coat on the peg, and he laid the morning's pine in the stove and brought it up, and he came around to the front of the bench and looked at Théodore and did not say anything, and Théodore looked back at him and did not say anything either, and Reuben went back to the stove. Reuben had been at Bailliard's since 1894 and he was sixty-one years old and he had a daughter in St. Louis and a son who had gone to the army in 1898 and not come back from Cuba, and he opened the shop in the morning and shuttered the shop in the evening and laid the fire and swept the floor and did the small clean work of the shop that did not require the maker's hand, and he had said three words to Théodore that morning between coming through the door and going to the stove, which had been *cold mornin Mr Bailliard*, and Théodore had said *cold*, and that had been the whole of it.

But Reuben had seen Théodore's face when he came in, and Reuben's father had been at Bailliard's in the time of Honoré, and had been the one to lay the fire in this same stove on the morning of October the fifteenth in the year of 1881, when Honoré had come in with his face the way Théodore's was now, and Reuben's father had told him about it some years afterward in a kitchen in the part of Natchez where they lived, and the telling of it had stayed with Reuben in the way the small private rememberings of a man's father stay with him through life, and Reuben laid the fire in the stove now with the bent careful motion of a man who has been told what a particular morning is going to look like and has lived to see it.

## II

Through October the sleep thinned. The first week he slept two hours a night, then less. By the second week of October he was sleeping for stretches of an hour at a time and then waking and lying in the dark with the clock at the foot of the bed counting the half hours, and by the third week he was sleeping for periods he could no longer measure with any precision, because the periods had become so short and so unrelieved that they were no longer separable from the waking, and a man who could not say with confidence whether he had been asleep for a quarter of an hour or had only been lying with his eyes shut was not, in any meaningful sense, asleep. The leaves on the pecans behind the house began to come down in the second week of October. The cane fires began at the edge of the bottomland the third week. The smell of the burning came up through the country the way it had been coming up through the country for fifty Octobers Théodore could remember and for some unknown number of Octobers before that, and Théodore took it in at the door of the shop in the mornings with a kind of cold attention he had not paid it before, because he had begun, in this autumn, to take in everything with a cold attention. A man whose sleep is being taken from him is given, in compensation, a clarity that is of no use to him, and Théodore had begun to understand the trade.

He kept his circuit. He wound the clock at Saint Mary's on the Mondays. He wound the clock at the courthouse on the Tuesdays. He wound the Carrington clock and the Lessley clock and the Ferriday clock and the long-case clock at the office of Mr. Pinckney Cole the lawyer on State Street, who had been his father's friend and was now his own. He wound the clock at the Natchez Hotel and the clock at the bank on Main Street and the small carriage clock at the office of Doctor Beauchamp on Pearl Street, and on the second Thursday of October he rode out the river road south to the Ravenel place at Bellechasse, where the long-case clock in the front hall had been keeping time since 1844 and had been wound by his father and his grandfather before him, and he was met on the gallery by the wife of Henri Ravenel, a woman of forty-seven named Cécile, who had known him for many years, and they exchanged a few sentences on the gallery, and he went in and wound the clock, and he came out, and he rode home before dark.

He noticed, over those weeks, that the clocks of Natchez had begun to disagree with each other. The clock at Saint Mary's, when he stood at the foot of its tower and listened, struck the hour at one stroke and the clock at the courthouse three blocks away struck it at another, and the difference between them was not a difference of a few seconds, which was natural, and to be expected in any town, but a difference of half a minute and once a difference of a full minute, which was not. The clock at the Carrington house struck four when he was setting it and the clock in his pocket said it was twelve minutes past three. He held his pocket watch up to the light and looked at it. He shook it. He held it to his ear. The watch was running. The watch had been running for fifty years and had been running well. He looked at the Carrington clock. He looked at the watch. He could not say which was wrong.

He said nothing of it to Mrs. Carrington. He said nothing of it to Reuben. He said nothing of it to Léonie. The clocks of Natchez were beginning to disagree with each other, and a clockmaker who could not say which clock was telling the time was a clockmaker who was no longer the maker of the time he kept, and the saying of this aloud would have been the beginning of a thing he was not prepared to begin yet.

Léonie, in the same weeks, began to wake at three in the morning.

She did not tell him. She had begun on a night in early October, on the night her own clock in her own ear told her that her husband had not slept since the lamp had gone out, and she had risen quietly from the bed and had gone down to the kitchen and lit the lamp and set the kettle on and sat at the table, and she had not made coffee that night, because the making of coffee would have been the beginning of a thing she was not prepared to begin yet either. She had sat at the table with the unlit kettle on the stove for an hour and a half and had then gone back upstairs to bed, and Théodore, who had been lying on his back with his hands folded on his chest above the blanket, had not given her any sign that he had heard her come and go.

The next night she had done the same. And the next. By the middle of October she had been waking in the small hours for fourteen consecutive nights, and she had begun, in the daytimes, to feel the kind of fatigue she had not felt since her own children had been small, although she and Théodore had had no children, the having of which they had decided against on the night before they were married for reasons Théodore had explained and Léonie had accepted and which neither of them had ever afterward referred to. The decision had been a thing that had passed between them once and had been done with. She had not, in the years since, regretted it. She did not, in the autumn of 1911, regret it. She had her husband. The years she had had were her own to count, and she was counting them.

In the third week of October, on the Friday after the Bellechasse trip, Théodore came home from the shop at six o'clock and stood in the front hall and could not at first remember the route by which he had come. He had walked the four blocks from Franklin Street to Pearl Street as he did every evening, and he had taken off his hat and hung it on the peg in the way he did every evening, and he had stood with his hand on the peg and tried to put together, in his mind, the sequence of streets between the shop and the front door, and he had not been able to do it. He had stood there for perhaps half a minute. Then Léonie had come out of the kitchen with her hands wet, and she had looked at him, and the route had come back to him, and he had nodded to her, and he had gone in to wash before supper.

He did not tell her, that night, what had happened in the front hall. He did not tell her on the next night, when he stood at the workbench at four in the afternoon and could not remember whether the clock he was holding in his hand had come in that morning or had come in the previous Tuesday. He did not tell her on the night when he stood in front of the unfinished clock at the back of the shop for what he believed to be five minutes and Reuben came in to say good night and Théodore looked up at the windows and the windows were dark, and Reuben said, in the soft careful voice of a man who has been thinking about how to say a thing he had not previously known how to say, *Mr Bailliard you been standin there since after the dinner hour*, and Théodore looked at him, and Reuben looked back at him, and Théodore said, *thank you Reuben*, and Reuben said, *yes sir*, and went out, and Théodore put on his hat and his coat and shuttered the shop himself and walked home in the dark, and the dark was the dark of a man who had stood in front of a clock without hands for six hours without knowing he had done it.

## III

The night his perception failed was the fifteenth of November, on a Wednesday, in the seventh week since the first thinning of his sleep.

By that night Léonie had been waking, alone, for forty consecutive nights, and had been sitting at the kitchen table for an hour or more in each of them, and had begun, on the thirty-second night, to make the coffee that she had not made on the first. She had not yet spoken to her husband about the waking. She had been preparing, through the forty nights, a sentence that she had not yet had any reason to say. She had revised the sentence many times. She had, on the night of the thirty-eighth waking, decided what the sentence was going to be, and the sentence was eleven words long, and she had been waiting since then for the night that would require it.

Théodore had, by that Wednesday, been sleeping no more than half an hour at a stretch and not more than two hours in any night, and the half hours had become, in his perception, indistinguishable from the wakings, and he could not have told a man who asked him whether he had slept the previous night. He had also, in those weeks, begun to lose his hold on the duration of things. He had stood in front of Joseph's clock for six hours without knowing he had done it. He had walked the four blocks home and not been able to recall the route. The duration of the walk and of the standing and of the lying in bed beside Léonie were no longer reliable to him. A minute and an hour had begun to feel the same in his chest, which was the chest of a clockmaker, and this was the worst of the things that were being done to him, because the duration of things had been his stock in trade since he was a boy.

On the morning of the fifteenth he went to the shop. He did not remember going to the shop. He found himself behind the bench with a clock in his hands and the front of the clock open, and he did not remember which clock it was or who had brought it in, and the morning light was in the windows, and Reuben was at the stove with his back turned, and Théodore did not call out to him, and Reuben did not call out to Théodore, and Théodore set the clock on the bench in front of him and looked at it, and the clock was a small mahogany mantel clock he had never seen before in his life. The card tied to the carrying handle gave the owner's name as Mrs. Lessley of Pearl Street, and the date on the card was October the twenty-third, which he had no memory of. He had been working on a clock, evidently, for twenty-three days, that he had no memory of taking in. He set the clock down. He sat at the bench. He put his hands flat on the wood on either side of the clock. He did not move for a while.

When he stood up the morning had become evening. The light in the windows was the cold yellow of a late November dusk, and Reuben was at the stove again, although Théodore did not know whether it was the same time he had been at it before, and the stove was lit, although Théodore could not recall its having been lit when he had sat down. He looked at his pocket watch. The watch said it was twenty minutes past four. He did not know whether the watch was right.

He went to the back of the shop. The unfinished clock stood in its corner, in the dim light from the back window, and he stood in front of it as he had been standing in front of it through October and November. The dial was bare. The dial had been bare for ninety years. The dial had hands on it.

The hands were brass. They were the hands of a clock, with the long minute hand and the short hour hand, and they had not been on the dial when he had last looked, which had been the previous night, or which had been the night before that, or which had been some night he could not name. The hands were moving. They were moving slowly, the way a slow clock moves, but they were moving, and there was no train behind the dial that could be moving them, because Joseph had never put one in. The hour hand was between two numerals. The minute hand was at a place that was not a recognizable minute mark. He stood in front of the clock for a length of time he did not measure. He did not touch the hands. He did not touch the dial. After some while he turned away from the clock and went back to the front of the shop, and Reuben was not at the stove any longer, and the shop was empty, and the windows were dark, and Théodore did not know whether Reuben had gone home or whether Reuben had been there when he had stood up, and he shuttered the shop himself, and he locked the door, and he went out into Franklin Street.

He did not go home. He turned east on Franklin and walked the two blocks to State Street and then turned south, and he walked along State Street to the bluff, and he came out at the head of the long iron stair that went down to Natchez-Under-the-Hill, and he stood at the top of the stair with his hands on the iron rail, and he looked down at the river. The river was black and wide and slow in the November night. The lamps of the boats at the wharf made small yellow bars on the water near the bank. There were voices coming up from the saloons at the foot of the stair, and they were the voices of white men talking in the language of white men in a saloon at the foot of Silver Street in the year 1911, and a man who has stood at the head of that stair in that year has heard those voices, and Théodore did not listen to the words.

He thought about going down the stair. He thought about going down the stair and walking through the lamp light at the foot of it and out along the wharf and to the end of the wharf, where the boards ended, and stepping off into the river. He thought about it the way a man thinks about a tool he has reached for many times in his life and has just now noticed in his hand. The river beneath the bluff was two hundred feet down and not less than a quarter of a mile across. He did not have to use the stair. He could step over the rail where he stood. The fall would do the work that the river would otherwise be asked to do.

He stood at the rail for what he afterward believed to be perhaps a quarter of an hour. The voices below came and went. A boat horn sounded once on the river and was answered by another farther down. Théodore did not move. He was thinking, in the time he stood there, of nothing he could afterward have given an account of. He was not thinking, particularly, of his father or his grandfather or his great-grandfather. He was not thinking of Joseph. He was not thinking of the clock with the hands on it. He was not thinking, even, of Léonie. He was thinking of the silence he had been listening for since he was nine years old, the silence after the third stroke, and he was thinking that the silence was very close to him now, closer than it had been all autumn, and that it would not require very much of him to enter it.

He turned away from the rail. He could not afterward have said why he turned away. He walked back along State Street to Pearl Street and turned north, and he walked the four blocks to the house, and there was light in the kitchen window at the side of the house, and he stopped on the brick path between the front gate and the front door and looked at the light for some while, and then he went around the side of the house to the kitchen door, and he opened the kitchen door, and his wife was at the table with the lamp lit, and the kettle was on the stove, and a cup of coffee stood at the place across from her, untouched.

She looked at him.

"Sit down, Théodore."

He sat down.

She did not move from her chair. She did not push the cup of coffee toward him. She sat across the table from him with her hands folded in her lap in the way her own mother had folded them at the same table, and she looked at him with the steady close attention of a woman who had been sitting at that table for forty consecutive nights waiting for the moment in which she would say to her husband the thing she had been preparing to say to him.

"I have been awake," she said. "I have been awake every night of this autumn that you have been awake. I have been at this table while you have been in the bed and I have been at this table while you have been at the bluff and I have been at this table while you have been wherever else you have been, and I will be at this table for as many nights as it takes. You are not to leave me alone in this house, Théodore. I have not spent twenty-eight years in it to be alone in it now."

She did not say anything else. She did not weep. He looked at her across the table for a long time. He did not move. The kettle on the stove began, after some minutes, to make the small first sound a kettle makes when its water is approaching the boil, and the sound came up between them, and Léonie did not turn to it. After a while Théodore reached across the table and laid his hand over hers, and she did not move her hand, and they sat that way until the kettle came to the full boil and Léonie rose and went to the stove and took it off, and she poured the water into the pot, and she came back to the table, and they drank the coffee together as the night thinned into morning at the windows.

## IV

He did not die in the autumn of his fifty-fourth year. He did not die in the autumn of his fifty-fifth year either, although the autumn of his fifty-fifth year was the autumn during which the long-case clock at the foot of his bed, which had been wound every Sunday by a Bailliard man for a hundred and thirty-two years, stopped on a Wednesday night in November of 1912 at thirteen minutes past ten o'clock, which had been the hour of Honoré's dying in November of 1881, and which was an hour Théodore had not known he was carrying in him until the clock stopped at it.

He took the clock down from the foot of the bed himself. He did this without speaking to Léonie. He carried it down the stairs, walking carefully because the case was tall and the brass weights had been removed by him before he carried it, and he set it in the corner of the parlor on a piece of folded canvas he had brought up from the kitchen, and he did not wind it that Sunday night, and he did not wind it the Sunday after that, and the clock stood in the corner of the parlor for the rest of his life and the rest of Léonie's life and was not wound again by either of them.

He kept his shop. He kept his circuit. He wound the clock at Saint Mary's on the Mondays and the courthouse clock on the Tuesdays and the Carrington and Lessley and Ferriday clocks on the Wednesdays and Fridays, and he wound the long-case clock at the Ravenel place at Bellechasse on the second Thursday of every month from the autumn of 1911 until the January of 1934, the month before he died. He did not, in any of those years, finish the clock his great-grandfather Joseph had begun in the back corner of the shop. The hands that had appeared on it in November of 1911 disappeared from it sometime in the spring of 1912, and Théodore did not see them go, and he did not, after they had gone, ever stand in front of the clock again for any length of time longer than the moment it took to pass it on his way to the back door.

He did not sleep again. From the autumn of 1911 until the February night in 1934 on which he died, he slept in the way of a man whose sleep had been taken from him in the autumn of his fifty-fourth year, which was to say that he lay down in bed beside his wife at the hour of the lamp, and he closed his eyes, and he did not afterward have any account of what happened in his head between that hour and the hour at which he rose the following morning, and the account he could not give was the account of a man who has not slept and does not know how he has passed the hours, and he passed twenty-two years of nights in this manner, and he became, in those years, a known figure at the rail above the bluff in the small hours, and the men of Natchez-Under-the-Hill who came out of the saloons at three and four in the morning saw him there and did not speak to him, and the watchmen who walked the streets of the old town at those hours saw him there and did not stop him, because a man who stands at the rail above the bluff for twenty-two years and does not go over the rail is no longer the kind of man for whom the watchmen are watching.

Léonie kept the lamp at the kitchen table. She had begun to light it on the first night of October of 1911, and she did not stop lighting it after the night of the fifteenth of November, and she did not stop lighting it after the autumn of his fifty-fifth year, and she did not stop lighting it after the spring in which the hands disappeared from Joseph's clock. She lit it every night. She lit it on the nights he was at the bluff and she lit it on the nights he was in the bed beside her, on the chance that he would go down to the kitchen instead of to the bluff, and she lit it on the nights he was in the chair in his own parlor beside the silent long-case clock, and she lit it on the nights when she did not know where he was and was not asking herself where he was. She lit the lamp on the kitchen table in the small hours of every night for twenty-two years, and the table sat under the lamp with two cups on it and the kettle on the stove warm if not hot, and a place was set for him whether or not he was there, and the table was the centre of her own long watch, which was the watch she had not known she was beginning when she had begun it.

Reuben kept the shop. He laid the fire in the morning and he shuttered it at night and he swept the floor and he did the small clean work of the shop that did not require the maker's hand, and he did this for the rest of his own life, which ended in 1924 of his heart on a Sunday afternoon in his own kitchen in the part of Natchez where he lived, and his daughter came down from St. Louis for the burying, and Théodore went to the burying and stood at the back of the church with his hat in his hand and did not say anything to anyone, and Léonie went with him, and they came home in the dusk and ate supper together and did not speak of the day. After Reuben was gone Théodore took on a younger man whose name was Charles, and Charles laid the fire and did the small clean work, and Charles was at the shop when Théodore did not come in on a Monday morning in February of 1934, and Charles was the one who walked up to Pearl Street to ask about it.

Théodore was in the bed his great-grandfather had brought up from Pointe Coupee in 1815, and he had pneumonia, and he had been in the bed for two days, and Léonie was in the chair beside the bed, and the doctor had been and gone and was coming again that evening. Théodore was seventy-six years old. He had outlived his father by twenty-two years. He had not slept in any of those years. He looked at his wife from the bed and he was thinking, in those minutes, of the kitchen table and the lamp and the forty nights at the beginning of it and the eight thousand and some nights since. Léonie had her hand on his hand on the blanket, and she did not move it, and he did not move his.

"Léonie," he said.

"Théodore."

"I have been awake."

"I know."

She held his hand. She did not weep. She had not wept in fifty years on this subject, and she did not begin in the small hour of a Monday afternoon in February of 1934 with the doctor due that evening and the lamp on the kitchen table waiting downstairs to be lit. He closed his eyes. He did not open them again. He did not die for some hours, but he did not afterward speak.

The clock at Saint Mary's struck eleven that night, and the clock at the courthouse struck eleven, and the clock at the Carrington house struck eleven, and the clock at the bank on Main Street struck eleven, and they struck within a few seconds of each other in the way the clocks of a town that is keeping its time strike the hour, because there were other men in Natchez who wound them now, and the clocks did what they had been made to do. The long-case clock in the corner of the parlor on Pearl Street, which had been silent for twenty-one years, did not strike. The unfinished clock in the back corner of the shop on Franklin Street, which had stood for one hundred and thirteen years without hands, did not strike either, and was sent the following month to a cousin of Léonie's in Pointe Coupee Parish, who took it in and put it in the corner of his own front room and did not finish it, and his children did not finish it, and his grandchildren did not finish it, and the clock stood in that house until it was taken out in the year 1972 and given to a museum in Baton Rouge, where it stood under a brass plaque in a glass case until the building it stood in was closed in the year 2003 for renovation, and the clock was put into storage, and was, at some point, lost.

The river beneath the bluff at Natchez goes on. The morning came up over the bluff on the Tuesday after Théodore's death, the way the morning had come up on the bluff every morning of his life, whether or not anyone in the houses above the river was waiting for it. Léonie lived seven more years. She lit the lamp on the kitchen table on the night he died, and on the nights of his wake, and on the nights of every February, and on the nights of every other month of every year that remained to her, and she sat at the table in her widow's clothes and drank her coffee, and she did not afterward have any wish to stop the lighting of it, although there was no one for whom it was being lit, and although she had begun, in those last seven years, to understand that the lighting of it had not, at any time in the previous twenty-two years, been only for him.
