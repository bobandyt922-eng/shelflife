# THE CANE FIRES

## Chapter One

By the autumn his son was killed, Henri Ravenel had not spoken to a Beaumont in forty-one years, and had not set foot on Beaumont land since the morning of his own father's funeral, when he had walked the fence line between Bellechasse and Glenmary and spit, once, in the dust on the Beaumont side of the wire, and had gone home and washed his mouth with whiskey and not spoken of it to anyone, then or since.

He was fifty-six years old in the October of 1916. He had the long bones and the dark coloring of his father's people, the Ravenels who had come up from Pointe Coupee in the 1790s, and his hair had gone iron grey at the temples but the rest of it had stayed black, and he wore it cut short in the manner of his father before him, which had been the manner of his grandfather before that. He was not a tall man, but he carried himself in a way that made strangers think he was taller than he was, and when he came into a room the people already in the room had a way of falling quiet without quite knowing why. He was a hard man. He had been hard since he was a boy. The country he lived in had taught him that what was not held tightly was lost, and what was lost did not come back, and he had taken the lesson into his blood when he was young and had never afterwards seen any reason to reconsider it.

He was sitting on the front gallery of Bellechasse, in the slat-backed chair that had been his father's, with a cup of coffee gone cold on the floor at his side, when his wife came out to tell him about the Tellman Place.

The house behind him was very quiet that morning. The girl who did the cooking, a woman of fifty whose grandmother had been a Ravenel slave and who answered to the name Tildy, was in the back kitchen with her hands in flour, and the only sound that reached the gallery from inside was the slow clock in the front hall that had been keeping time in the house since 1844 and that no one alive had ever heard wrong. The fields below the house were still green at the edges and white at the tops, and the cotton would be in by the end of the month. The river was not visible from the gallery, but a man who knew where to look could see, in the gap between two pecan trees down the slope, the place where the cottonwood broke and the brown of the river began. Henri had been looking at that gap for an hour without seeing it. He was thinking, as he often did at this hour of the morning, of nothing in particular, by which is meant only that he was thinking of the same things he had thought of every morning for forty years, and had ceased to notice that he was thinking of them.

Cécile came out without speaking. She set her hand on his shoulder for a moment and then took it away, and she went and sat in the chair across from him, which had been her mother-in-law's chair, and which she had not sat in for the first twelve years of her marriage and had begun to sit in only after Henri's mother died in 1894. She was a small woman with very fair skin and grey eyes, and her hair was still mostly its old color, a faded blonde that the sun had bleached almost white at the front. She had been raised across the river in Pointe Coupee Parish in a house full of women, and she had brought into her marriage a particular kind of quiet that was not the quiet of submission but the quiet of a woman who had decided, very early, what she would and would not say aloud, and who had held to those decisions through thirty-four years of weather.

"Tellman is dead," she said.

He looked at her. "When."

"Last night, Tildy says. Her sister works in the kitchen up there. She came down this morning to bring it."

"Was it the heart."

"She doesn't know. They found him in the chair on his own gallery."

Henri took up the cold coffee and drank the bottom of it and set the cup back down. He was quiet for a long time. He was looking at the gap between the pecan trees again, but now he was seeing it.

"Augustus will sell," he said finally.

"I expect he will."

"He has no use for that land."

"No."

"He has not had a use for it in twenty years. He keeps it because Tellman pays his rent on time and because his father told him not to sell off any more of what we sold him. That is the only reason."

Cécile said nothing. She had heard him say a version of these sentences many times over the years, on the rare occasions when the Tellman Place came up between them, which was perhaps once or twice a year and never for long. The Tellman Place was four hundred acres of bottomland adjoining Bellechasse on its northern side, lying between Bellechasse and the Beaumont place at Glenmary, eight miles up the river road toward Natchez, and it had been Ravenel land from 1822 to 1872, and had passed to the Beaumonts in a transaction Henri's father had never afterwards discussed, and had been leased to a man named Tellman from 1874 onward. Tellman had been a stranger in the county when he came. He had married a Mississippi woman and raised four children on the place and had died, evidently, on his front gallery in a chair, in the same week the cotton was coming in for the forty-third time under his hand.

"I want it back," Henri said.

She had known he would say it. She had known it as she had walked from the kitchen to the gallery with the news in her mouth. She had known it for ten years, since the time Tellman had first mentioned to a man at the cotton exchange that he was getting tired, and the remark had been carried back to Bellechasse by another man, and Henri had heard it and said nothing and gone out to the barn and not come back until dark. She had known it before that, even, in a deeper way. There had never been a year in their marriage in which Henri had not been waiting, with the patience of a man who has nothing else to do with his patience, for the day this conversation would arrive.

"How," she said.

"I will go to the bank."

"What will you offer."

"Whatever it takes."

"Henri."

"Whatever it takes, Cécile. We will find it."

She did not answer at once. She was looking at her hands in her lap. Her hands were small and freckled across the backs and the knuckles had begun to swell in the last two winters, and she had begun to be ashamed of them, although she did not say so to her husband and would not have said so to her daughter. After a moment she lifted her head.

"It is not in our hands to find."

"Then we will find it elsewhere."

"From whom. Tell me from whom. Pointe Coupee will not give it to us, you know they will not. My brother gave us what he had to give, in 1908. There is no one else."

"There is the bank."

"The bank holds Beaumont paper, Henri. You know that. The bank in Natchez is half Beaumont. Augustus's cousin sits on the board."

"There are other banks."

"In Vicksburg, maybe. In New Orleans. Yes. And what are we to them, in Vicksburg or New Orleans. We are a name in a ledger. They will lend us nothing on this place that we cannot repay in three good seasons, and we have not had three good seasons in a row since Pierre was a boy."

He was quiet again. He had heard her, and he did not disagree with what she had said, but the disagreeing was not the point and never had been. She knew this. She had learned it in the second year of her marriage and had never forgotten it.

"I will go to Natchez tomorrow," he said. "I will see what can be done."

"Henri."

"What."

"Pierre."

"Pierre what."

"Will you tell him."

He looked at her. The sun had come up over the line of cottonwoods at the river and was striking the gallery now at its eastern end, and the light caught the side of his face, and she saw, in the way she had been seeing for some years, how much of his father was in him. His father had sat in this same chair until the day in 1903 when he had been carried into the house and put to bed and had not come out of the bed alive. His father had carried the grudge whole, the way a man carries a stone he has decided not to set down. Henri had taken the stone from him, on the day of the burying, by walking the fence line and spitting in the dust. He had taken it cleanly, as one takes an inheritance, and he had carried it since.

"He knows already," Henri said.

"He does not know you mean to do it."

"He will know by tonight."

"And you will not tell him first."

"I will tell them at supper, Cécile. Pierre and Sylvie at the table. Étienne in his room after. We are a family. This is a thing the family will know together."

She nodded. She did not press him further, because she knew that pressing him on it would only harden him, and she had not raised herself from a girl in Pointe Coupee to a woman of fifty-two by being a fool about her husband. She rose from her chair, and she touched his shoulder again as she passed him, and she went back into the house. The clock in the hall struck the half hour as she passed it. It was nine-thirty in the morning, on the sixth of October, in the year 1916.

Henri sat on the gallery for another hour and a half. He did not move except once, to send Tildy's grandson, who was eleven and ran errands for the household, down to the barn to tell the man there to have the buggy ready at first light the next morning, and to put up oats for a day's drive. The boy went down the path at a run and came back at a walk. Henri thanked him and gave him a nickel and the boy went around to the kitchen to give the nickel to his grandmother for keeping. Henri sat on, and the morning lengthened, and at eleven o'clock he rose from the chair and went down the steps and walked, slowly, around the house and out past the smokehouse and across the yard to the place where the east wing had stood before it burned in November of 1864, when he had been four years old and had seen the fire from his mother's arms in the upper window of the standing wing. The bricks of the foundation were still there in the grass, sunk now and grown over, and a man who did not know what they were would have walked across them and not seen them. Henri stopped at the corner of the foundation and stood there for a while. He did not know, himself, why he had walked out to it. He had walked out to it perhaps a thousand times in his life. It was a thing he did. After a few minutes he turned and walked back to the house.

The midday meal was eaten quietly. Pierre had ridden out before breakfast to look at a fence on the south line and had not yet come in. Henri took his place at the table with Cécile and Sylvie, and Cécile said the grace, and the three of them ate in the easy half-silence of a family who had taken a thousand meals together in the same room, and Henri did not say what he was waiting to say. He had decided, walking back from the foundation, that he would not say it twice. He would say it once, with all of them at the table together, and have it done. Sylvie asked her mother a small question about the church on Sunday, and her mother answered, and Henri ate, and after the plates were cleared he went into the small office at the front of the house and shut the door, and he sat at the desk and looked at the books for the rest of the afternoon without entering a figure.

Pierre came in at five, with the dust of the road on him, and went up to wash. Cécile went out to the kitchen to speak with Tildy about the meal. Sylvie set the table for supper. The clock in the front hall struck six and the light at the windows had begun to thicken into the long blue of an October evening when the family came into the dining room and took their places, and Cécile said the grace, and the food was brought in, and Henri laid down his knife on the cloth beside his plate and looked the length of the table at his wife, who was looking back at him, and then he looked at his son and his daughter together, in the way a man looks at the two children he has left when he is about to say a thing to them that he has already said in his head a hundred times.

"Tellman is dead," he said. "I am going to Natchez in the morning to see about buying back the place."

Pierre, who had been reaching for the salt, stopped his hand in the air.

"From the Beaumonts," he said.

"From the Beaumonts."

Pierre set down his hand. He did not pick up the salt. He sat back in his chair, slowly, and he looked at his father, and his face had gone still in the way faces went still in that family when something important had been said and the listening was being done.

Sylvie did not understand at first. She had heard the name Tellman before and knew the place by sight from the road, but the sentence about the Beaumonts had gone past her, and she looked from her father to her brother and back, and saw Pierre's face, and then she understood that she did not understand, and she set her fork down on her plate and put her hands in her lap and waited.

"Father," Pierre said.

"Eat," Henri said. "We will speak of it after."

"And Uncle Étienne," Sylvie said, in the small clear voice she used when she was not sure whether she was permitted to speak.

Henri looked at her. "I will go to him after the meal. I will tell him myself. He has a right to it before any of us goes to bed."

"Yes, Father."

The meal that followed was a long one and very nearly silent. When the plates had been cleared and Cécile had risen to see to the kitchen, Henri rose with her and went down the front hall to the back room at the end of it, and he opened the door and went in and shut it behind him, and the lamp was burning on the table beside the bed and old Étienne was awake against the pillows and looking at the door before it had finished opening. Henri sat down in the chair beside the bed. He told it short. He said that Tellman was dead, and that he was going into Natchez in the morning, and that he meant to have the place back. He did not say more than that. Étienne heard him out without speaking. When Henri had finished, Étienne looked at him for a moment and then closed his eyes and opened them again, and he said only, "I have been waiting to hear that sentence for forty-one years, Henri. Go and do what you mean to do." Henri sat with him a moment longer. Then he rose and went out of the room and shut the door behind him.

The speaking of it with Pierre, when it came, did not come at the table. It came on the gallery in the dark, after Sylvie had gone to bed and Cécile had gone in to sit with old Étienne in the back room, and it was only the two of them in the chairs, the father and the son, with a single lamp on the rail and the river invisible below the bluff and the cane fires beginning to show, here and there, at the far edge of the bottomland, where the first of the harvest had begun to be burned.

## Chapter Two

The lamp on the rail was a small brass lamp with a chimney that had been cracked since the previous winter and had been mended with a strip of tin and a length of fine wire by the man who kept the barn, whose name was Lucius and who had been at Bellechasse since before Pierre was born. The mend had held for nine months, which was longer than anyone in the house had expected. Henri had said, when he had first seen it, that Lucius could mend anything that was made of metal and several things that were not, and it had become a small joke in the family, the kind of joke that gets repeated because there is comfort in the repetition. The lamp threw a soft yellow circle on the boards of the gallery and on the lower half of Pierre's face where he sat across from his father, and his eyes were in the dark above it, and Henri could not see them.

"I want to understand," Pierre said.

"Then ask."

"You have not spoken his name in this house since I was a boy."

"I know what I have not done."

"You have not spoken his name and now you are going into Natchez tomorrow morning to sit in a room with his lawyer and ask him to sell you a piece of land."

"I am not asking him. I am offering to buy. There is a difference."

"To him there will not be a difference."

Henri did not answer at once. He had taken out his pipe after supper, and he was working at it now, packing the bowl with the slow economy of a man who had been packing pipes for forty years and could do it in the dark. The smell of the tobacco came up between them, heavy and sweet, and it covered, for a moment, the colder smell of the river coming up from below the bluff.

"You think I should not do it," Henri said.

"I think you should think about what you are doing."

"I have thought about it."

"For how long."

"Longer than you have been alive, Pierre. Longer than your mother has been in this house. I have thought about that piece of land every day of my life that I have been old enough to know it was not ours. There is no thinking left in it. There is only the doing."

Pierre was quiet. He was looking at his hands, which were resting on the arms of the chair, and which were his father's hands in miniature, the same long fingers, the same flat shape to the nails. He had been told, on more than one occasion in his life, by old men in Natchez and by his father's cousins from across the river, that he was the image of his grandfather, and he had taken the saying of it without comment, because he had learned young that there was nothing to be gained by remarking upon a thing that other people had decided was true.

"They will not sell to you," he said.

"Perhaps not."

"They will not sell to you, Father. He will not sell to you. Augustus Beaumont will sell that place to a Chinaman before he sells it to a Ravenel."

"That is possible."

"Then why."

"Because the place is going to be sold, and a Ravenel must offer for it. That is the whole of it. If he refuses, he refuses, and the refusing is on his head and not ours. But he will not refuse without having been asked, and the asking is what I am going to do. I have waited forty-one years to ask, since the summer I was fifteen and saw what I saw on the road below Pinewood. I am not going to be turned aside from the asking by a son who has decided, on a Friday evening in October, that the asking is undignified."

"I did not say it was undignified."

"You meant it."

"I meant that it will cost you. That it will cost us."

"It will cost us what it costs us."

"And if he says no."

"Then I will go to a man in Vicksburg who has been a friend of your uncle's for twenty years, and I will see what can be done about borrowing against the cotton, and I will go to him a second time, and I will offer Beaumont more than the place is worth, and a third time if I have to. There is a price at which a man like Augustus Beaumont will sell his own grave, Pierre. I am going to find it."

The lamp flickered. A moth had come up out of the dark and was beating itself against the chimney, and the small repeated sound of its body against the glass was, for a moment, the only sound on the gallery. Henri reached up and brushed it away with the back of his hand. It went off into the dark and did not come back.

"You have not asked me," Pierre said, "whether I want it."

"I have not."

"Why not."

"Because you are a Ravenel, and there is no Ravenel in five generations who would not want it back. I do not need to ask you that. The asking would be an insult to your blood."

"There are men in this country who walk away from what their fathers wanted. There are sons who do that."

"There are."

"And you have decided I am not one of them."

"I have decided nothing about you, Pierre. You will decide that for yourself. I am telling you only what I am going to do tomorrow morning, and what I am going to do in the weeks after, until the thing is done one way or the other. You may decide what you want to do about it. You are twenty-two years old. You are old enough to make your own choices about your father."

Pierre laughed then, a short laugh without humor in it, and he leaned forward into the circle of the lamp, and Henri saw his face, and the face was tired, and there was a thing in it Henri did not at first recognize. Pierre had been at the place all of his life. He had ridden the fence lines with Lucius when he was nine. He had taken his first deer in the bottoms when he was twelve. He had gone for two years to a small college in Tennessee that his mother's people had paid for, and had come home in the spring of 1914 with two years of Latin that he had not opened a book in since, and had taken up the running of the cotton operation under his father's hand the way a man takes up a tool that has been waiting on a bench for him to come of age. He had never, in any of those years, given Henri reason to think he would walk away from anything.

"I am not walking away," Pierre said. "I am asking."

"Then I have answered."

"You have answered some of it."

"Then ask the rest."

Pierre was quiet for a moment longer. Then he said, "What was she like."

Henri did not move.

"You have never told me," Pierre said. "Mother told me the story when I was nine, the way her mother had told it to her, for her people in Pointe Coupee had been distant kin to the Ravenels there before the war and had carried our griefs as their own. Old Étienne told me a piece of it once when I was fifteen, and stopped, and would not finish. You have told me nothing. You have spoken her name perhaps four times in my life. I would like to know what she was like."

"I did not know her."

"You knew of her."

"I knew of her the way you know of her. I was three years old when she died. I did not know her. My father spoke of her sometimes. My mother spoke of her never. Étienne knew her. Étienne was twenty-eight that year and home at Bellechasse and saw it. There is no one else."

"Then tell me what your father said."

Henri set down the pipe on the rail beside the lamp. He did not look at his son. He looked out into the dark, in the direction of the cane fires, of which there were now three visible on the far edge of the bottomland, three small orange smudges against the black. The cane was a syrup crop, planted in the low wet rows that would not take cotton, and it was being burned in the trash rows after the cutting, and the smoke of it would come up through the country for the next six weeks, and the smell would settle into the wool of every coat in Adams County.

"He said she was kind," Henri said. "He said she sang in the evenings. He said she had a small dog she had named for a saint, and the dog was killed in the same year she was, but not on the same night, and not in the same way. He said she had been promised to a man, and that the man was the son of a neighbor, and that the neighbor's family was a family we had been on good terms with for thirty years before the war. He said that on the night the foragers came to Bellechasse, the man she was promised to was at Bellechasse also, and that he saw what was done to her. He said the man had not done enough. That is all my father told me. He told me these things on five or six occasions across forty years, and never the same things on the same night, and never all of them in a row. I have put them together myself, the way you put a piece of broken pottery together when you have found it in a field. Whether the promise had been broken before Marguerite Beaumont became his wife, no one in either house ever said, and no one in either house knows now."

"And the man she was promised to."

"Charles Beaumont."

"Augustus Beaumont's father."

"Yes."

"Who is now eighteen years dead."

"Yes."

"And whose son is going to refuse to sell you that land tomorrow."

"Yes."

Pierre nodded slowly. He had heard the names before. He had even, in his own way, put together the same broken pottery, although his pieces had come from his mother and from his great-uncle and not from his father, and his arrangement of them was therefore slightly different. He had been carrying the arrangement in his head since he was about thirteen. He had not, until this moment, sat in a chair across from his father and been given the version his father had made.

"I will go with you tomorrow," Pierre said.

"No."

"Father."

"I said no, Pierre. You will not go with me. You will stay here, and you will see to the cotton, and you will look after your mother and your sister and your great-uncle. I am going alone. There are some things a man does alone, and the asking back of his family's land is one of them. If I take you with me I am telling Augustus Beaumont that I do not believe I can speak for the Ravenels by myself. I will not say that to him. I will not say it to him with my words and I will not say it to him with my company. Do you understand me."

"Yes."

"Then we are finished with this for tonight."

Pierre rose from the chair. He stood for a moment looking down at his father, and Henri did not look up at him, and after that moment Pierre stepped past the lamp and went to the screen door of the gallery and opened it and went inside. The screen door slapped shut behind him. Henri sat alone with the lamp and the smell of the tobacco and the small orange smudges of the cane fires at the far edge of the night, and he sat there for another hour, and at the end of the hour he reached up and turned down the wick of the lamp and the gallery went dark, and he sat in the dark for a while longer, and after that he got up and went into the house and went to bed beside his wife, who was awake but did not speak to him, and he slept, eventually, the way he had slept every night of his married life, which was poorly and not for long.

In the back room at the end of the downstairs hall, old Étienne was awake. He was awake because the pain had been waking him every night for a month at about this hour, and because the laudanum that the doctor had left on the small table by the bed was nearly gone, and because he was conserving it now against the worse nights he knew were coming. He lay on his back in the bed his great-grandfather had brought up from New Orleans in 1817, and he listened to the house. He had lived in the house for seventy-eight of his eighty-one years. He could place every sound in it. He had heard Pierre come in from the gallery. He had heard Henri come in an hour later. He had heard Cécile cross the upstairs hall to the bedroom and not cross it back. He had heard the clock strike eleven and then half past and then twelve. He had heard the small wind come up out of the southwest and move through the pecan trees and die again.

He had heard, also, the conversation on the gallery, although he had not heard the words. The words did not carry. But the shape of the conversation had carried, the way the shape of any father-and-son conversation about a hard thing will carry through the floor of an old house, and he had lain in his bed and followed the shape of it with the close attention of a man who had nothing left to do with his attention but listen.

He knew what Henri was going to do. He had known it for ten years, the same way Cécile had known it. He had known it longer than that, in fact. He had known it since the day Henri had come back from his own father's burying with his mouth still tasting of the whiskey he had used to wash it.

He turned his head on the pillow and looked at the small table where the laudanum bottle stood. There was perhaps an inch of the thick brown liquid left in the bottom of it. The doctor was coming again on Tuesday. Étienne had decided, in the quiet way he decided most things, that he would ask for a larger bottle this time, and that he would not say why.

There was a thing he was going to have to do before he died, and the doing of it was going to require more nights of being awake than he had thought, when he had first understood that he was dying, that he was going to be given. He had not yet decided to whom he was going to do it. He had narrowed the candidates to three, in his own mind, over the past several weeks. He had narrowed them, after the last narrowing, to two. Tonight, lying in the dark and listening to the house breathe around him, he held them at two, and turned the choice between them over once, and once again, and let it lie.

He closed his eyes. He did not sleep. He waited for the morning, in the patient way of the dying, who have learned that morning comes whether or not a man waits for it, and that the waiting is the only thing left in the world that belongs to them entirely.

## Chapter Three

A week later, on Saturday morning the fourteenth of October, Natchez smelled of horses and of the river and of the fires of the cotton seed presses down at the foot of Silver Street, where the gins ran from before dawn until past dark in this season and the air for two blocks around them was thick with the lint dust that came up out of the bagging house and settled on the shoulders of every man who walked past. The town sat on the bluff above the river, two hundred feet up, in two parts: the old town on the heights, with its courthouses and banks and churches and the long shaded avenues of houses with white columns and iron fences, and Natchez-Under-the-Hill at the foot of the bluff, which was the part of town a respectable person did not mention by name in mixed company, and which had been, since before the war, the part of town where the rougher business of the river was done. The two parts of Natchez had no use for each other and pretended, in 1916 as in 1850, that they did not share the same name.

The cotton exchange stood on Franklin Street, a block off Main, in a yellow brick building with a green-painted door and a brass handle that had been polished by the hands of every cotton man in Adams County for sixty years. Camille Beaumont had been to the cotton exchange perhaps twenty times in her life, always with her father, and she had a fondness for the building that her father did not know about and that she would not have been able to explain to him if he had asked. There was a smell inside the front door that she liked. It was a smell of old varnish and tobacco and the cologne the older men wore, and underneath it the faint dry smell of the cotton itself, which had been carried in on the coats and in the satchels of forty years' worth of men coming and going. The smell had been there in her childhood and it was there still, and she had come to think of it as the smell of business being done, and to find a small comfort in that.

She had come into Natchez with her father on the seven o'clock buggy that morning. Augustus had a meeting with two men from Memphis, and her mother had given her a list of things to be picked up in town, and Camille had taken the list and the small silk purse with her household money in it, and had ridden in beside her father in the buggy without speaking, in the easy way they had with each other on the road. Augustus did not require his daughter to make conversation when there was no conversation to be made. It was one of the few things she loved unreservedly about him. She was twenty years old and had been her father's daughter long enough to know which of the things she loved about him she could love without reservation, and which she could not.

He had let her down at the corner of Franklin and Main and gone on to the exchange ahead of her, and she had done two of the errands on her mother's list at a dressmaker's shop and at a stationer's, and she had then walked back along Franklin to meet her father at the exchange, where she would wait in the small front room while he finished his business. She had been doing this since she was twelve. The men at the exchange knew her, and were courteous to her in the elaborate way of men of that place and time, and the older among them had been giving her butterscotches from a tin in a side drawer of the desk in the front room since she was a child. She did not particularly want a butterscotch this morning. She wanted to sit in the chair by the window and watch the street and wait for her father to be finished, and then to go home.

She came up to the green door at twenty minutes after ten. The door had been propped open with a brass weight on account of the warmth of the morning, and as she came up the two stone steps a young man came out of the door at the same moment, going down, and they nearly walked into each other in the doorway. He stopped. She stopped. He took a half step back into the building and held the door open for her with his right hand and lifted his hat with his left, and he said, "I beg your pardon, ma'am."

She said, "Not at all."

He smiled. It was a small smile, and brief, and tired around the edges, and it was the kind of smile a man gives a woman he has not been introduced to and does not expect to see again, and to whom he wishes to convey, in the small space of a doorway in a public building on a Saturday morning, that he has noticed her without wishing to be ungentlemanly about the noticing. She returned the smile with a nod and went past him into the front room, and he lifted his hat once more, fully this time, and went down the steps and turned right on Franklin and walked away up the street.

She sat in the chair by the window. She set her purse in her lap. She watched the street. She did not at once think about the young man, although she had taken in, in the brief moment of his standing in the doorway, that he was perhaps two or three years older than she was, and dark-haired, and not from Natchez, or at any rate not from any part of Natchez she knew, and that there was a small white scar above his right eyebrow that ran up into the hairline and was old, the kind of scar a boy gets from a fall at six or seven and carries the rest of his life. She thought of all of these things only in the way a person thinks about a stranger she has crossed at a doorway, which is to say not at all consciously, and as a kind of background to the other thinking she was doing. She was thinking, mostly, about whether she would have time after her father was finished to walk down to the bookseller's at the foot of State Street and look at what had come in.

She had been sitting there for perhaps a quarter of an hour when one of the older men came into the front room from the back, a man named Mr. Ellery who had known her father since before she was born, and who stopped at the desk to put down a ledger he was carrying, and who looked up and saw her and said, "Miss Beaumont. Your father will be another thirty minutes, I believe. Will you take a coffee."

"No, thank you, Mr. Ellery."

"There was a young man here this morning, but he came in just as another piece of business was finishing, and he stepped out and did not wait."

"I expect I passed him in the doorway."

"You may have. A young Ravenel. He had come in for cotton — the family runs eighty acres in cotton at Bellechasse and brings the bales in themselves — and he had been told to come at ten and came nearer to twenty past, and the price clerk had stepped out, and the boy did not have the patience to wait, which I do not blame him for at his age."

She had not been expecting the name. She had been expecting any of a dozen names, perhaps, of young men her father might have business with on a Saturday morning, but she had not been expecting that one. She did not let her face change. She had been a Beaumont long enough not to let her face change. She said, in the same tone she had been using for the previous remark, "Ah. I had thought he was a stranger."

"He is not exactly a stranger to your family, Miss Beaumont, though he is a young man and your father may not have laid eyes on him before. The Ravenels and your family have not had occasion. He is the only son. We have done business with the family on cotton matters here for fifteen years, in a small way, and they pay their bills."

"I see."

"Will you take a coffee, after all."

"No, thank you, Mr. Ellery. I am content."

He nodded, and went back into the rear of the building, and she sat in the chair by the window with her purse in her lap and the small silk strap of it wound, now, around her two middle fingers, although she had not been aware of the winding until she felt the silk beginning to bind. She unwound it. She set the purse on the windowsill beside her. She was looking out at the street.

She could see, at the far end of Franklin, the corner of the building where the street turned up toward Main, and she watched that corner for a moment without quite knowing she was watching it, and then she turned her face away from the window and looked at the brass nail-heads on the back of the chair across from hers, and she counted them, and there were twenty-six of them in three rows of eight and a fourth row of two.

She did not know why she had counted them. She had not intended to.

When her father came out of the back room forty minutes later, with two men from Memphis behind him whom he introduced to her by name and who lifted their hats and said the right things and went on out the door, Augustus Beaumont put on his hat and offered her his arm and they went out together into the street.

He helped her up into the buggy, and went around and climbed up beside her, and took the reins, and they went out of Natchez along the river road in the bright cool noon light, and the cotton was open in the fields on either side of the road, and there were Black men and Black women and their children working in the rows with long sacks over their shoulders, and the white was coming out of the bolls in the slow steady way it came out in October, and on the way out Augustus said, in the comfortable rambling way he talked when he was on the road and had eaten a good lunch, "I had a young Ravenel in the building this morning, on cotton. Ellery saw to him, or did not, depending on how you look at it. The boy went away without waiting. His father is a hard man, Camille. He has put a foolish thing in his head about the Tellman Place that I will not encourage. There has been some correspondence on it that you do not need to know about. The boy has nothing to do with it. He came in for the cotton on a Saturday morning the way any cotton man does. There are forms that must be observed in these matters. We are not animals."

"No, Father."

"Did you finish your errands."

"Yes, Father. I have a parcel from the dressmaker's here beside me."

"Good."

He went on talking, in the same comfortable rambling way, about the men from Memphis and what they had wanted and what he had told them, and Camille listened, and made the small sounds at the right places, and watched the road, and did not, for the rest of the ride home, allow herself to think the name she had been told that morning, although the name had been sitting at the back of her mind from the moment Mr. Ellery had said it, and was sitting there still when the buggy turned up the long drive to Glenmary in the early afternoon, and the white columns came up out of the green of the trees, and her mother came out onto the gallery to meet them.

She got down from the buggy. She kissed her mother on the cheek. She handed her the parcel from the dressmaker's. She went up the front steps and into the cool of the front hall, and she crossed the hall and went up the stairs, and she went into her room at the head of the stairs and shut the door behind her, and she sat down on the edge of the bed, and she put her hands flat on the counterpane on either side of her, and she said, aloud, in the empty room, his name.

*Pierre Ravenel.*

She had not heard the first name from Mr. Ellery. She had pieced it together on the ride home, from things her father had said about the family at the dinner table over the years, and from things her grandmother had said, and from one thing in particular her brother Henry had said, two summers ago, about a Ravenel boy who had come back from a college in Tennessee and was riding the fence at Bellechasse with the old man's man Lucius, and who, Henry had said, with the small unattractive sneer he reserved for the Ravenels, *thinks he is going to be something*.

She sat on the edge of the bed for some while. Then she rose and went to the window and looked out at the bluff and the river, and at the road that came up from Natchez along the bluff, and she stood there a long time, and after that she went down to help her mother with the parcels and with the supper, and she said nothing about anything to anyone for the rest of that day.

## Chapter Four

The first letter had come on a Wednesday, three days before the Saturday morning at the cotton exchange. Henri had ridden into Natchez the previous Saturday and come home with no answer in his mouth, and on the Tuesday he had put the offer in writing and had Lucius carry it in. The letter that came back was carried out from Natchez by a Black boy on a mule who had been sent by the law office of Mr. Pinckney Cole, who had been Augustus Beaumont's lawyer for eighteen years and his father's lawyer before that, and the boy gave the letter into the hand of Henri Ravenel at the front gate of Bellechasse and was given a dime for the carrying and was offered water at the kitchen, which he took, and he sat on the back step in the shade and drank two cups of it and ate a piece of cornbread Tildy gave him, and he rode back to Natchez in the heat of the afternoon. The letter was not long. Henri read it standing at the gate with the boy still drinking water at the kitchen, and then he read it a second time more slowly, and he folded it and put it into the inside pocket of his coat and walked up to the house.

The letter said that Mr. Augustus Beaumont had received Mr. Henri Ravenel's communication of the previous Tuesday, and that Mr. Beaumont had given the matter due consideration, and that Mr. Beaumont was not at this time prepared to entertain an offer for the parcel known as the Tellman Place, but that Mr. Beaumont wished it understood that no slight was intended toward Mr. Ravenel personally, and that Mr. Beaumont remained, as ever, his neighbor's faithful servant. It was signed by Mr. Cole on behalf of Mr. Beaumont. The hand was Mr. Cole's. The phrasing was Mr. Cole's. The decision was not.

Henri sat at the desk in the small room at the front of the house that had been his father's office and that he used now for the keeping of the books, and he laid the letter flat on the blotter and looked at it for a long while, and then he took up his pen and wrote a second letter. The second letter was longer than the first he had sent. It thanked Mr. Beaumont through Mr. Cole for the courtesy of a reply, and it stated that Mr. Ravenel was pleased to learn that no slight had been intended, and it asked Mr. Beaumont to reconsider, in light of the long history of the parcel and of the families adjacent to it, whether some accommodation might not be reached on terms to be discussed. It did not name a price. It did not need to. It was, in its way, a polite and very deliberate refusal to accept the first refusal, and any man who read it would understand that what Henri Ravenel was saying, beneath the words, was that he intended to be heard out fully before he would consent to be turned aside.

He sealed the letter. He gave it to Lucius the next morning to carry into Natchez. Lucius came back at noon with a receipt from Mr. Cole's clerk and nothing else.

The second letter from Mr. Cole came on the following Monday. It was shorter than the first. It said that Mr. Beaumont had received Mr. Ravenel's further communication and had considered it, and that Mr. Beaumont's position was unchanged, and that Mr. Beaumont must respectfully decline any further correspondence on the subject. It was signed by Mr. Cole. It was, in the courteous code of the country, a slammed door.

Henri read it at his desk and put it in the same drawer with the first one. He did not write a third letter. He sat at the desk for an hour. Then he stood up and walked through the house and out the back and across the yard to the barn, and he had Lucius saddle a horse for him, and he rode out by himself across the fields to the river road and turned north along it, and he rode the sixteen miles to Natchez at a slow walk, and he came into town in the late afternoon and he went to the office of Mr. Cole on State Street, and he asked the clerk if Mr. Cole was in.

The clerk was a young man of twenty-five named Albert Pritchard, and he had been with Mr. Cole for two years, and he knew the name Ravenel in the abstract way a young man new to a town knows the names of the people his employer does business with, and he did not at first understand who was standing in front of him. He said Mr. Cole was indeed in, and would Mr. Ravenel care to wait, and he went into the back office to announce the visitor, and there was a silence behind the door that lasted longer than Henri had expected, and then Mr. Cole came out himself, in his shirtsleeves and his vest, with his coat over his arm, and he said, "Henri," in the voice of a man who has been caught at something he had hoped to avoid.

"Pinckney."

"Will you come back."

"I will."

They went into the back office. Mr. Cole shut the door. He did not put on his coat. He sat down behind his desk and he did not invite Henri to sit, and Henri did not sit, and they regarded each other across the desk in the late afternoon light from the one window, which faced west and was throwing the long bars of the venetian blind across the floor between them.

"I have come about the letters," Henri said.

"I assumed you had."

"I want to know if it is your decision or his."

"You know it is not mine, Henri. I am his lawyer. I write what he tells me to write."

"I want to know what he told you to write."

"I have written it. You have it in your hand."

"I want to know what he said to you, Pinckney. Privately. In this office. When he sat where I am standing now and told you what to say to me. I want to know whether he gave a reason."

Mr. Cole looked at him for a long moment. He had known Henri Ravenel for thirty years, and he had been, in the quiet way of country lawyers, fond of him, and he had been embarrassed by the part he had been asked to play in this matter from the moment Augustus Beaumont had walked into the office the week before and laid out his instructions. He had not been at liberty to say so. He was, even now, not at liberty to say so. He weighed, briefly, what he could say without saying so.

"He gave a reason, Henri. I will not tell you what it was. I will tell you that it was not a reason that any man in this county would think well of him for, and that I told him as much, and that he heard me out and did not change his mind. That is what I can say to you. I cannot say more."

"It is enough."

"Henri."

"What."

"Will you sit down."

"No, Pinckney. Thank you. I will not sit down. I came to ask the question and you have answered it. I will not sit down in the room where the answer was given."

Mr. Cole nodded slowly. He understood. He had expected something like it. He stood, in his shirtsleeves, and he came out from behind his desk and he held out his hand, and Henri took it, and they shook once, and Henri turned and went out of the office, and the clerk Pritchard saw him go, and Henri rode home in the dark.

He did not tell his wife, that night, about the visit to Mr. Cole. He did not tell his son. He told no one. He went to bed and lay in the dark and listened to the house around him, and he did not sleep until almost morning, and when he came down to breakfast Cécile saw his face and did not ask, because she had been married to him for thirty-four years and knew, by now, when not to ask.

He did not stop, however. He had told her, on the morning after the gallery talk, that there were things to be tried beyond Natchez, and he had not described them, and in the days that followed the visit to Mr. Cole he began to make the descriptions real. He sat with the cotton ledgers on three successive evenings, in the small office at the front of the house with the door shut, and he worked out in his clean small hand what the place could carry against a loan if the season held, and what it could not. He drafted a letter to a man in Vicksburg whom Cécile's brother had spoken of, who had been a friend of the family for twenty years and had once said, in a parlor in Pointe Coupee, that he would do for a Ravenel what he would not do for any man in Mississippi. He folded the letter and held it for two days before he sealed it. He drafted a second letter to be sent to Mr. Cole, in Augustus Beaumont's name, in which a price was set down for the first time, and the price was a number that would have astonished any man in Adams County who had heard it spoken aloud. He sealed the second envelope on the Monday morning. He set it on the corner of his desk. He had not yet sent either of the two letters on the Tuesday afternoon, and by the Tuesday evening they had become a different kind of object, and were not sent at all.

It was in the same weeks that the second meeting between Pierre and Camille occurred, and the third.

The second was at the courthouse, two days after Henri's ride into Natchez. Pierre had been sent in to file a paper with the clerk on a matter of his father's that had nothing to do with the Tellman Place, and Camille had been brought in by her mother to sit with her grandmother Marguerite, who was giving testimony in a small dispute over a will of a cousin's, and the two young people passed each other in the upper corridor between the registrar's office and the courtroom, and there was nobody in the corridor with them. Pierre stopped. Camille stopped. He took off his hat. He said, "Miss Beaumont." She said, "Mr. Ravenel." He said, "I had not known your name when I held the door for you the other morning." She said, "I had not known yours either." He said, "I am sorry for the foolishness of our families." She said, "So am I." He said, "Good day, Miss Beaumont." She said, "Good day, Mr. Ravenel." He put his hat back on and went past her toward the registrar's office, and she went past him toward the courtroom, and they did not look back at each other, and neither of them spoke of the meeting to anyone, and the entire exchange had taken less than thirty seconds.

The third was on the road outside the Methodist church on the following Sunday morning. It was a coincidence and not a coincidence. The Beaumonts attended the Methodist church in Natchez and the Ravenels did not, but a cousin of Cécile's was being baptized that morning at a Methodist church on the river road at Pinewood, which lay between Glenmary and Bellechasse, three miles below the Beaumont land and five miles above the Ravenel place, and the Ravenels had come up from Bellechasse for the service, and Pierre had gone outside before the closing hymn to wait by the buggies because the closeness of the building had begun to give him a headache. The Beaumonts had attended their own service in town and had gone on afterward to call on the Hardimans, whose place lay below Glenmary near Pinewood, and they had stopped along the road on the way home to finish a piece of cotton business Augustus had begun in the Hardiman parlor, and the buggies were drawn up under the trees, and Camille had got down to walk in the shade for a moment while her father talked, and she had walked perhaps a hundred feet up the road and had come around a bend and there was Pierre standing alone beside a pair of horses tied to a hitching rail outside a small white church she had not known was there.

He saw her. She saw him. There was nobody else on the road for fifty yards in either direction.

She did not turn around. Neither did he. She walked the rest of the way to where he stood, and stopped at a proper distance from him, perhaps eight feet, and they looked at each other for the third time in nine days, and this time neither of them said anything for some while.

"My father is in the church," he said at last.

"My father is up the road. With the Hardimans."

"Yes."

"I came down to walk."

"I came out for the air."

"I did not know there was a church here."

"It is a small one. My mother's cousin is being baptized. She married a man from up here last spring."

"I see."

He was looking at her. She was looking at him. Neither of them was smiling. The light was coming down through the leaves of the live oaks above the road, and the light was cool and yellow and broken, and the two horses tied to the rail were quiet, and somewhere in the church behind him people were singing the closing hymn, and the singing came out through the open windows of the church and across the small graveyard and into the road where the two of them were standing.

"I should not be here," she said.

"No."

"Neither should you."

"No."

"And yet we are."

"Yes."

She did not move. He did not move. After what seemed to her a long time but was perhaps twenty seconds, she said, "Mr. Ravenel."

"Miss Beaumont."

"I want to ask you a thing and I do not know if it is proper to ask it."

"You may ask."

"Do you believe what your family believes about what happened in the war."

He did not answer at once. He had not expected the question. He had not, in fact, expected any question. He had expected the two of them to stand in the road for as long as they dared, and to part again, and to say nothing of consequence to each other. He had been wrong.

"I believe," he said slowly, "that my great-aunt is dead, and that she died young, and that the family I was raised in carries her death the way a house carries a beam that is rotted through. I believe that the carrying is real. I do not know, anymore, whether what is being carried is what they think they are carrying. I have been thinking about this for some years, Miss Beaumont, and I have not arrived anywhere with the thinking."

She nodded. She was looking down at the dust of the road at her feet.

"My grandmother," she said, "tells the story differently than your family does."

"I know."

"You have heard it."

"I have heard pieces of it."

"It is not a story I believe, exactly. I do not know what I believe. My grandmother tells it as though she were there. She was twenty-five years old in 1863. She was at Glenmary the night your great-aunt died. She has been telling the story since I was a child."

"Yes."

"I do not believe my grandfather did what your family says he did."

"My family does not say that he did it himself."

"I know."

"My family says he allowed it."

"I know that too."

He looked away from her, up the road. The Hardiman buggy was visible at the far end of it, perhaps three hundred yards distant, and the figure of her father and the figure of Mr. Hardiman were standing in the road beside it, and they had not yet finished their talk.

"I do not believe your grandfather allowed it either," he said. "I have not, for some time, believed it. I have not said so to my father. I have not said so to anyone, Miss Beaumont. I am saying it to you now because I have stood on this road with you and I do not see, when I look at you, the granddaughter of a man who would allow a woman to be killed."

She did not look up. She was quiet for what felt to him a long time.

"Thank you," she said.

"You are welcome."

"I think I will go back to my father now."

"Yes."

She lifted her face and looked at him once more, briefly, and then she turned and walked back up the road in the broken yellow light, and he watched her until she had gone around the bend, and after that he turned and went back to his horse, and he untied it and stood beside it and waited for the church doors to open and for his father and his mother and his sister to come out, and he gave no sign on his face that anything had happened, and when his family came out of the church and Sylvie asked him whether he had been all right out in the air, he said he had, and the headache was almost gone, and they got into the buggy and they drove home.

She did not tell anyone about the meeting on the road. She had not told anyone about the courthouse either, and she did not now. Her father, in the buggy on the way home, asked her if she had enjoyed her walk, and she said she had, and he said it was a pretty stretch of road, and she agreed that it was, and that was all.

Old Étienne, that night, asked Sylvie a question.

Sylvie had been the one to bring him his supper, on a tray, as she had been bringing it to him for two months now, since he had stopped coming to the table. She set the tray on the small bedside table and helped him sit up against the pillows, and she fed him small spoons of the broth that was almost all he could keep down anymore, and she sat with him afterward while he drank water, and she had risen to go when he had said her name and asked her, in the soft careful voice he used now for everything, to sit down again for a moment.

She sat down.

"Sylvie," he said.

"Yes, Uncle."

"How is your brother."

"He is well, I think. Why."

"Has he spoken to you of anything in the past week."

She thought. She was sixteen years old. She had been her brother's confidante since she was nine. He had not, in fact, spoken to her of anything in the past week, which was unusual.

"No, Uncle. He has not."

"He has been quiet."

"He has been a little quiet, perhaps. Father has been quiet too. The thing about the land has gone badly, and I think they both feel it."

"It is not the land, Sylvie."

She looked at him.

"What do you mean."

He did not answer at once. He was looking at her in the dim light of the lamp on the table beside the bed, and his eyes, which had gone milky around the edges in the last two years, were on her face with a closeness that was not familiar to her, and she felt, for a moment, the cold small feeling a person feels when an old relative they love begins to look at them in a new way.

"I mean only that there are other things on his mind. Watch him for me, Sylvie. Be kind to him. Do not press him on anything. If he wishes to speak to you, listen. That is all I ask."

"Yes, Uncle. Of course."

"Good girl."

He closed his eyes. She sat for another moment, and then she rose and took the tray and went out, and she went down the hall to the kitchen and gave the tray to Tildy, and she went up to her room. She did not understand what her great-uncle had asked of her, or rather she understood the words but did not understand why he had used them. She did not know that he had been watching, in the quiet patient way of the dying, the small changes in her brother over the past two weeks, and that he had drawn from those changes a conclusion that no one else in the house had drawn, and that he had narrowed his three candidates to two, and then to one, and that the one was now her brother Pierre, and that the watching of Pierre, from the back room at the end of the downstairs hall, had become the principal occupation of the small remainder of his life.

## Chapter Five

Henry Beaumont came home from Natchez on the evening of the twenty-eighth of October, and he was drunk when he came home, in the steady controlled way he had begun to be drunk in the past year, the way of a young man who had decided, without admitting to himself that he had decided, that the whiskey was a thing he was going to have every day for the rest of his life and had therefore worked out how to carry it without falling off his horse. He came up the drive at Glenmary at half past seven in the evening, and he gave his horse to the boy at the stable and he came up to the house and into the front hall, and he hung up his hat on the peg by the door, and he turned to go into the parlor where his mother and father were sitting before supper, and his sister Camille came down the stairs at that moment, and he stopped in the hall and looked at her.

"Camille."

"Henry."

"I had a thing told to me in Natchez today."

"Did you."

"I had it told to me by Tom Carrington at the bar of the Natchez Hotel, in the presence of two men I did not know, and I did not contradict him because I did not know how to contradict him, and I have been thinking about it for the last three hours."

She had stopped on the second step from the bottom. She kept her hand on the railing. She did not come down the rest of the way.

"What did Tom Carrington tell you, Henry."

"He told me he had seen you, on the river road, last Sunday, in the company of Pierre Ravenel. Outside the Methodist church at Pinewood. He told me he had passed by in his buggy and seen the two of you standing in the road together. He told it as a small thing. He told it the way a man tells a thing he thinks the other man already knows. I am asking you, Camille. Was it true."

She did not answer at once. She had known, at the courthouse in Natchez, that there had been no one in the corridor. She had known, on the river road, that the Hardiman buggy had been three hundred yards distant. She had not, in either case, accounted for the possibility of a buggy passing on the road behind her, in the brief moments before her father's came around the bend. She saw, now, that she should have. She saw, also, that she had not because she had not, in either of those moments, been thinking about being seen. She had been thinking, both times, about the man in front of her.

"It was true," she said.

"It was true."

"Yes, Henry."

He did not move. He was still standing in the hall with one hand on the peg where he had hung his hat. The voices from the parlor were a low murmur through the closed parlor door. Their mother was telling their father something about the cook, in the easy domestic register of a woman who did not yet know that her son had come into the front hall.

"What were you doing with him, Camille."

"I was speaking to him."

"About what."

"It does not concern you."

"It concerns me very much."

"Henry."

"It concerns me, Camille, because I am your brother, and because that man is a Ravenel, and because there is no version of you having a conversation with a Ravenel on the river road that I am prepared to accept as a thing that does not concern me. What were you talking about."

"I will not tell you."

"You will."

"I will not, Henry. Lower your voice."

He took a step toward the stairs. She did not retreat. He stopped at the foot of the stairs and looked up at her, and his face was the face of their father in twenty years, and she saw it, and she had been seeing it for two years now, and the seeing of it had been part of the small private griefs of her life.

"Camille. Listen to me. That man's family has been calling our grandfather a murderer for fifty years. Did you know that. They say he led men to Bellechasse in the war and stood by while their woman was killed. They have said it in their own kitchen for half a century. And you stand on a road with that man's son and let him tell you he does not believe our grandfather. You believe him over your own people."

"I have heard the story all my life. As you have."

"And you stood on a road with him."

"I stood on a road with a man I had met, by accident, twice before. We spoke for a few minutes. He told me he did not believe his family's version of what happened. I told him I did not believe ours. We parted. That is the whole of it, Henry. There was no more to it than that. There has been no more to it than that."

"You spoke to him."

"Yes."

"You let him say to you that our grandfather was a liar."

"He did not say that."

"He said our grandfather did not allow it. That is the same thing."

"Henry."

"Did you let him kiss you."

"Henry."

"Did you."

"Lower your voice, you fool. They will hear you."

"Did. He. Kiss. You."

"No. He did not. He has not. He did not so much as touch my hand. He stood eight feet from me on a public road and we spoke for a few minutes and then I walked back to our father. That is what happened, Henry. That is all that happened. Now get away from the stairs and go upstairs and wash your face before you come into the parlor, because Mother will smell you the moment you walk through that door."

He stood at the foot of the stairs for a moment longer. Then he turned away. He did not go upstairs. He went into the parlor, with the smell of him going in front of him, and the parlor door shut behind him, and Camille came down the last two steps of the stairs and stood in the front hall for a long moment with her hand still on the railing, and she did not go into the parlor. She turned and went back upstairs to her room and shut the door and sat down on the edge of the bed and she did not cry, because she had made a decision about crying when she was twelve years old and had not gone back on it since, but she sat for a long time with her hands flat on the counterpane on either side of her, and her breath came short in her throat for a while, and after a while it steadied.

She came down to supper a quarter of an hour later, and she sat across from her brother at the table, and she did not look at him, and he did not look at her, and their parents and their grandmother saw nothing of it, or saw it and chose not to. The supper was eaten. The plates were cleared. Camille went up to her room and Henry went out onto the back gallery with a glass and the bottle.

He did not, in fact, ride into town. He rode south on the river road, away from Natchez, in the direction of Bellechasse.

He did not have a clear idea, when he set out, of what he was going to do. He had an idea that he was going to find Pierre Ravenel. He had an idea that he was going to say certain things to him. He had an idea that the saying of those things would make him feel a certain way that he had not been feeling for some weeks now, and that the feeling that way would settle in him whatever it was that had not been settled by his sister's account in the front hall the night before. He met Pierre Ravenel on the road three miles south of Glenmary, at the place where the river road bent away from the bluff and went briefly inland through a stand of pines. "Ravenel."

"You are blocking the road."

"I am."

"I will not."

He took in, last and not least, the look on Henry's face, which was a look he had seen on the faces of other young men of that country at other times in his life, and which he knew the meaning of, and he made, quickly and without seeming to, the calculation he had to make.

"No."

## Chapter Six

He waited through the next day and into the morning after that.

He waited because he was sober on the morning after, for the first time in a month, and the soberness frightened him more than the drink had, and he understood, in the small clear way he understood things when the whiskey was out of him, that what he was preparing to do was not a thing he had believed he could do sober. He had decided, in the hour on the bed, that he was going to do this sober, because he had also decided, in the same hour, that the doing of it was a thing his grandfather would have understood, and that if he was going to do a thing his grandfather would have understood he was going to do it the way his grandfather would have done it, which was sober and in daylight and on his own land or as near to his own land as he could manage. He waited for forty minutes.

The rifle fired.

"I do."

"You believe I have known for fifty-three years. You believe I have not told."

"I have always believed it. My father believed it. He never asked you. I have wanted to ask you since I was twenty years old. I have not asked you because I did not want to take from you the only thing you had ever held back from this family, and because the holding back of it seemed to me a thing you had earned the right to."

Étienne lay quiet for a moment. He looked at his great-nephew. The lamp on the small table beside the bed threw the light up onto the underside of Henri's chin and made the lines there harder than they were in daylight, and made his eyes look black.

"Henri."

"Uncle."

"Do you want me to tell you now."

Henri did not answer at once. He sat with his hands folded between his knees and looked at the floor. He sat that way for what was, by the clock in the front hall, perhaps three minutes, and during those three minutes Étienne did not move or speak, and the small flame of the lamp on the table burned without flicker, and the house around them was as quiet as a house can be in the deep middle of the night.

"No," Henri said.

"No."

"No, Uncle. I do not want to know."

"Tell me why."

"Because I have killed my son with the version I have. If the version I have is wrong, then I have killed him for a lie, and I cannot bear that, Uncle. I cannot bear it. I would rather die with the version I have. I would rather be a man who lost his son to a true thing than a man who lost his son to a thing that was not what he thought it was. Do you understand me."

"I understand you."

"Then do not tell me."

"I will not tell you, Henri."

"Thank you."

"You are welcome."

Henri sat for a while longer. Then he reached out and took his uncle's hand, which lay on the blanket above his chest, and he held it, and Étienne let him hold it, and they sat that way for some time.

"There is a letter under my pillow," Étienne said at last.

Henri did not move.

"Uncle."

"It is a letter I wrote this week. It contains the thing you have asked me not to tell you. I will keep it under my pillow until I die. I do not know whether that will be next week or in three months. When I die, you may do as you wish with the letter. You may read it. You may burn it. You may put it in a drawer for your daughter to find when she is sixty years old. It is yours to decide. I am telling you now that it is there so that you will know, when I am gone, that the choice has been left to you."

"Uncle."

"Yes."

"I will burn it."

"I thought you might."

"I will burn it without reading it."

"Then it will be ash, and the ash will go up the chimney, and the truth of what happened at Bellechasse on the night of October the seventh, in the year 1863, will go up the chimney with it. There will be no one left who knows. The story will be what your family has said it was. The story will be what the Beaumonts have said it was. The two stories will go on in the two houses, and they will be all that is left, and after a generation or two no one will care whether either of them is true. That is what will happen, Henri. I want you to know what you are choosing."

"I know what I am choosing."

"Then choose it."

"I have."

Étienne closed his eyes. He did not speak again that night. Henri sat with him for another hour, holding his hand, and then he rose and went out of the room and shut the door, and he went into the bedroom at the front of the house, where his wife and his daughter were sitting beside the body of his son, and he sat down with them, and the three of them sat together until first light.

Étienne died eight days later, in the small dark hour before dawn on a Wednesday morning. Sylvie was with him. She had been sleeping in a chair beside his bed for the last three nights. She did not see him go. She woke at six o'clock. The room was very still. She looked at him. He was gone. She went and woke her mother. Cécile came in. Cécile stood beside the bed and looked at the old man. She closed his eyes. She turned to Sylvie and took her into her arms, and they stood together for a long minute, and then Cécile lifted the pillow from beneath his head, and the letter was there, folded, with no name on the outside of it. She picked it up. She did not open it. She gave it to Sylvie.

"Take this to your father."

Sylvie took it.

She found her father on the front gallery. He was sitting in the slat-backed chair that had been his father's. He had not slept well in nine days. She came out onto the gallery and stood beside the chair and held out the letter.

"He is gone, Father."

"I know. I heard you on the stairs."

"He left this. Mother said to give it to you."

He took it from her. He did not look at it. He held it in his right hand against his thigh.

"Sylvie."

"Yes."

"Go in to your mother."

She went.

He sat for some while with the letter in his hand. Then he rose from the chair and walked across the gallery and down the steps and across the yard, and he went into the kitchen, where Tildy had already started the morning fire in the stove, and he opened the front of the stove with the iron handle, and he put the letter in. He closed the stove. He stood for a moment with his hand on the warm iron of the door, and then he turned and went out of the kitchen and back across the yard and up the gallery steps, and he sat down again in the chair, and he looked out at the bottomland, where the cane fires were still burning at the far edge, although not as many of them as there had been a month ago, because the cutting was almost finished now, and the year was almost finished, and the smoke was thin and pale in the cool morning air.

At Glenmary that same morning, eight miles to the north, Camille Beaumont was sitting on the front gallery alone. She had begun to sit on the gallery in the early mornings since the night her father had come home from Natchez without Henry, on the day after the killing. Henry was in the jail in Natchez awaiting his trial, which would be held in the spring. Augustus had not been to see him after the first week, and would not go again. Marguerite had taken to her bed and would not come down. Eleanor moved through the house in the way she had always moved through the house, doing the things that needed to be done, and she did not speak of her son to anyone, and she had stopped going to church.

Camille sat on the gallery. She did not read, although she had brought a book out with her. She sat and looked at the bluff, and at the river beyond it, and at the road that came up from the south.

She had not told her father about the conversation in the road outside the church at Pinewood. She had not had to. Henri Ravenel had told him, in the front hall, on the night Pierre died. The next morning, Augustus had taken Henry into Natchez himself, and had come home in the afternoon without his son, and had said almost nothing to anyone for the rest of that day. That night he had gone up to the gun room and had taken down the rifle and had laid it on the table in the front hall and had stood looking at it, and her mother had said "Augustus," and her father had said nothing, and after a while her father had picked up the rifle and put it back on the rack, and the sheriff sent a deputy for it the following afternoon. Her father had asked her, the morning after that, in his office, with the door shut, whether what Mr. Ravenel had said was true. She had told him it was. She had told him everything. He had heard her out without interrupting. When she had finished, he had said only that he was sorry, and that he should have known and he had not, and that he was not angry with her, and that he did not blame her for any of it, and that if she had it to do over she should not change a thing she had done, because she had not done any wrong, and that the wrong had been done by his own father and his own son, by a Beaumont in the war and by a Beaumont fifty-three years later, and that he would carry it for the rest of his life and that she should not.

She had thanked him. She had gone back upstairs.

She sat now on the gallery in the cool of the morning. She thought of the young man she had met in a doorway, and spoken to in a corridor, and stood with on a road in the broken yellow light. She did not know, sitting on the gallery on the morning Étienne Ravenel died eight miles to the south, that the letter had just been burned. She did not know that there had been a letter. She did not know, and would never know, that the version of the story her grandmother had been telling her since she was a child was not the true version, and that the true version had gone up the chimney of the kitchen at Bellechasse in the cold smoke of a morning fire. She knew only that there had been a young man, and that there had been three meetings, and that the three meetings had come to a smile in a doorway and fewer words, all told, than a single page would hold, and that those few words and that one smile had been, although she had not understood it at the time and would understand it only over the slow years that followed, the largest thing that had ever happened to her, or that ever would. She was not mourning a man she had loved. She was mourning the brief moment in a doorway in October when another life had seemed possible to her, and the closing of that door behind her on a road outside a small white church she had not known was there.

She would not marry. She would live in the house at Glenmary until she was an old woman, and she would care for her mother, and after her mother for her father, and she would bury both of them, and she would inherit the house and the name, and she would be the last Beaumont at Glenmary, and the house would pass at her death to a cousin's son who lived in Memphis and had not been to Natchez since he was twelve. She would not be unhappy. She would be quiet. She would read in the evenings on the front gallery in the chair her grandmother had sat in. She would not go to Bellechasse, although Bellechasse stood for the rest of her life, and Sylvie Ravenel lived in it until she died of influenza in 1934, and the house and the land passed then to a nephew in New Orleans who sold it the following year. The Tellman Place was sold by the Beaumonts in 1923 to a man from Memphis who farmed it for thirty years and whose son sold it to a paper company in 1957. The river road was paved in 1929. The cotton exchange on Franklin Street was closed in 1942 and the building stood empty until 1958, when it was torn down to make room for a bank. The Methodist church at Pinewood burned in a lightning storm in 1947 and was not rebuilt. The bluff at Glenmary stands. The river beneath it goes on.

The cane fires of October, 1916, burned for six weeks across the bottomland between Bellechasse and Glenmary, and they were seen from both galleries, by the people who remained in both houses, and the smoke of them came up through the country and into the wool of every coat in Adams County, as it had come up every October for fifty years before that and as it would come up every October for some years after. The smell of it was a smell of an ending. It had always been a smell of an ending. The people who lived in the country knew this, and accepted it, in the patient way of people who live close to land that takes more from them than it gives, and who have learned, over the long course of their lives, that there are some things in this world that no one alive remembers the beginning of, and no one alive will live to see the end of, and that a man may do, in the course of one autumn, a thing whose consequences will outlast every person who knew his name.
